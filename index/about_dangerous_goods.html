What are Dangerous Goods
What are Dangerous Goods
    Dangerous goods are substances that meet the UN Recommendations. Dangerous goods are allocated a class according to its properties.
    Classes of Dangerous Goods

    Each type of dangerous good is allocated a United Nations (UN) number. A complete list of dangerous goods is found in Volume 1 of the Australian Dangerous Goods Code.

    Packing Groups
    Packing Groups
      Each substance is allocated a packing group. These groups indicate the level of risk involved in transporting the goods.

      Risk factors include:
      • Toxicity
      • Flashpoints
      • Quantity

      There are three packing groups. These are:
      Packing Group I (PGI)
      • Great Danger
      Packing Group II (PGII)
      • Medium Danger
      Packing Group III (PGIII)
      • Minor Danger

Load volumes classed as Dangerous Goods
Load volumes classed as Dangerous Goods
    Customers should be advised to contact the manufacturer of the product for information about the substance classification as a dangerous good.

    A Dangerous Goods Vehicle Licence is required when the package or container's capacity exceedes 500 litres or 500 kilograms.

    Note:Capacity is based on the total volume of the container not the amount of substance put into it. Therefore even if the container is only 1/2 full (or emptied), the vehicle still requires a Dangerous Goods Vehicle Licence.
Dangerous Goods Placarding
Dangerous Goods Placarding
    A placard load is a load that requires Class labels and emergency information panels to be positioned on a vehicle in particular configurations.

    Refer to :
    • Australian Dangerous Goods Code Vol 2. Chapter 5.3

    Label TypePurpose
    Class LabelDiamond shaped label indicating the class of dangerous good
    Subsidiary Risk PlacardIndicates additional dangerous properties held by the good being transported. (Not required in all cases)
    Emergency Information PanelIdentifies the method of dealing with the goods in the event of an emergency

    Aggregate Quantity Explained
    Aggregate Quantity Explained
      The aggregate quantity is the total of the:
      • Number of kilograms of solid dangerous goods and aerosols
      • Number of litres/kilograms of liquid dangerous goods recorded on the transport documentation
      • Total capacity in litres of containers with Class 2 (excluding aerosols) dangerous goods
DefinitionsPackaged dangerous goods are goods transported in smaller containers such as drums, bags, bottles or packets.

An IBC is a
    • Rigid or flexible portable package
    • Is designed to be handled and transported without damage when loaded
    • Complies with chapter 6.5 of the ADG Code
