Warrants to Enter (5267)


Statement
The Transport Operations (Road Use Management) Act, Transport Operations (Passenger Transport) Act and the Heavy Vehicle National Law Act 2012, allows an authorised officer to apply to a Magistrate for a warrant to enter a place.
  • The authorised officer must prepare a written application stating the grounds on which the warrant is sought.
  • The written application must be sworn.
Note:The authorised warrant official may refuse to consider the application until all the information the official requires about the application is supplied in the way the official requires.

Generally a warrant must state:
  • The place to which the warrant applies;
  • That the stated authorised officer or any authorised officer may, with necessary and reasonable help and force:
    • Enter the place and any other place necessary for entry to the place;
      and
    • Exercise the authorised officer’s powers
  • Particulars of the offence that the authorised warrant official considers appropriate in the circumstances;
  • The name of the person suspected of having committed the offence unless the name is unknown or the authorised warrant official considers it inappropriate to state the name;
  • The evidence that may be seized under the warrant;
  • The hours of the day or night when the place may be entered;
  • The authorised warrant official’s name;
  • The date and time of the warrant’s issue;
  • The day, within 7 - 14 days (dependant under what legislation the warrant is issued) after the warrant’s issue, the warrant ends.
Note:
  • When it is determined that a warrant is likely to be sought, a brief must be prepared for sign off by the relevant RD.
  • This brief is to be copied to GM Customer Services Branch, GM Land Transport Safety, GM Passenger Transport Services, Deputy D-G and D-G.
  • Unless there are exceptional circumstances (i.e. significant evidence of possible public safety risks) it is desirable that this brief will be made available at least 2 - 3 weeks ahead of the actual serving of the warrant. Issues of major significance should be briefed ahead of this.
  • A further short update of the brief is to be provided 24 hours ahead of the warrant execution.
  • Further briefs are to be provided on the progress of the matter on such issues as company reaction, media or other involvement (TWU, QTA etc.)

At the same time, an authorised officer should also prepare a warrant to enter which can be signed by the Magistrate if he/she agrees with your application.

As it is necessary for the authorised officer to make an appointment to see a Magistrate, the authorised officer should prepare the application and also a warrant to enter for presentation to the magistrate. If the Magistrate agrees with your application, he/she will sign the warrant to enter.

Important Points
1.Never try to walk in off the street to see a Magistrate. Always make an appointment first. Explain to the person whom you are making the appointment what it is in relation to.
2.When attending the appointment with the Magistrate, always be neatly attired and act professionally. Always refer to the Magistrate as "YOUR WORSHIP" regardless of how they may introduce themselves. It is also common practice to give a short bow when entering and leaving the Magistrates chambers which is more than likely where the Magistrate will issue the warrant.
3.Have the relevant legislation with you, preferably marked at the relevant pages for quick reference.
4.Be prepared to explain exactly what you are intending to do and why. If you are relying on confidential information as the basis for your complaint, the Magistrate may require you to divulge your source of information. If you are relying on documentation, have it with you. It may help if you explain to the Magistrate what the effects relating to the commission of the offence are, whether it be road infrastructure damage, liability relating to unregistered vehicles, danger to other road users caused by overloaded vehicles or whatever. It may also assist to explain that you are wanting to "go after" the cause of the relevant problem (this may be the employer) rather than the sometimes "innocent" driver or that you believe that this is the only way you can obtain the evidence necessary for prosecution.
5.Have the relevant application and warrant/s to enter with you, neatly typed and ensure that the information contained therein is accurate. It would help to have two copies of each in case of errors etc. (See para 12).
6.It may be worth noting that in these days of computerisation, the information you require may be on hard disc and for this reason it is sometimes appropriate to include the words "and/or data" in the description of the search object. If you believe that this may be the case, it is suggested that you utilise the services of your local Computer Officer and have them attend at the execution of the warrant with necessary discs etc. for downloading data. An alternative is to have the information you require printed out in hard copy, however there are times when this is not practicable because of time constraints. It is suggested that the Computer Officer carries out this work unless you are a computer expert yourself as this may negate allegations of damage to the system and also ensure that you get all of the relevant information.
7.Before entering the premises described in the warrant, ensure that all Transport Inspectors attending are carrying concealed tape recorders fitted with fresh tapes and batteries, and that they are turned on prior to entry. Videotaping also plays a role with the execution of warrants to enter. If videotaping please ensure that all procedures explained in the training program are complied with. In these instances, this procedure may save you from allegations of misconduct and also you may tape spoken evidence which may be used in court at a later date. Please remember that the video camera is also an audible recorder and you must be aware that anything said is to be in accordance with the judges’ rules otherwise all the video evidence may be thrown out. Remember, you may not have to execute the warrant at all. It is suggested that you identify yourself by showing appropriate identification and then advise the person in charge what it is that you are investigating. At times, if you ask for the required information, you may find that it is supplied without having to resort to the execution of a warrant to enter which has the benefits of reducing or eliminating the "big brother" attitude of those we deal with, and also it is one less matter to be dealt with in court, should that situation arise. Should consent be forthcoming, it is imperative that the consent be recorded in some manner to avoid allegations later that the evidence was seized illegally.
8.Dependent upon circumstances, it is suggested that police accompany Department of Transport and Main Roads (TMR) Inspectors when executing warrants to enter. This has the benefits of providing additional safety for Inspectors, police can corroborate details of the execution or otherwise of the warrants and, at times, a police presence can engender a spirit of cooperation with potential defendants which may otherwise not exist.
9.If the person named in the warrant or the authorised person in the case of a company asks to see the warrant, it is suggested that this be allowed to occur, however, UNDER NO CIRCUMSTANCES ALLOW THE PERSON TO TAKE POSSESSION OF THE WARRANT as they may attempt to destroy it. A copy of the warrant can be given to the person concerned, for their information, however there is no requirement in law to do so, and this course of action is at the discretion of the Inspector concerned.
10.When access is gained and the search commenced, it is strongly suggested that a complete search of the premises described in the warrant take place, even if you think that you have found what it is that you are searching for. Dependant on the legislation concerned, you may uncover evidence of other offences under the same legislation of which you were not aware. Naturally this evidence must fall within the definition of the search object in the warrant to enter.
11.Some legislation does not require the supply of a receipt for seized evidence, however it is probably not a bad idea to supply one in any case as it provides you with a record of what you seized including dates and times.
12.Prior to describing the 'search object' in the warrant and application, it may be helpful to elementise the offence or offences described in the warrant as this may assist in identifying exactly what the search object should be. It may also assist in assessing if the evidence seized covers all of the elements of the offence/s or if additional evidence is required.
13.Particular notice should be taken of the relevant legislation you are working under, in relation to the seizing and returning of evidence. Most legislation imposes specific time frames in which to institute proceedings and return evidence should proceedings not be commenced. In other words, READ THE APPLICABLE LEGISLATION VERY CAREFULLY, NOTING IMPORTANT POINTS, PRIOR TO DOING ANYTHING.
