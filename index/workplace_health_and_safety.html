Workplace Health and Safety (7398)


Workplace Health and Safety
WHS ObligationsThe Queensland Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (WHS Act) provides a framework to protect the health, safety and welfare of all workers at work and of all other people who might be affected by the work. The WHS Act also provides protection for the general public so that their health and safety is not placed at risk by work activities.

The WHS Act aims to: -
  • Protect the health and safety of workers and other people by eliminating or reducing workplace risks
  • Ensure effective representation, consultation and cooperation to address health and safety issues in the workplace
  • Encourage unions and employers to take a constructive role in improving health and safety practices
  • Promote information, education and training on health and safety
  • Provide effective compliance and enforcement measures
  • Deliver continuous improvement and progressively higher standards of health and safety

Throughout the WHS Act, the meaning of health includes psychological health as well as physical health.

The WHS Act places the primary health and safety duty on a person conducting a business or undertaking (PCBU). Transport and Main Roads (TMR) as a government entity is considered to be PCBU. The PCBU must ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health and safety of workers at the workplace. Duties are also placed on officers of the PCBU, all workers and other persons at the workplace.

The WHS Act sets out the requirements relating to: -
  • Incident notification
  • Consultation with workers
  • Issue resolution
  • Inspector powers and functions
  • Offences and penalties

While at work, workers are required to take reasonable care for their own health and safety and that of others who may be affected by their actions or omissions. They must also cooperate with any reasonable instruction given by the PCBU and any reasonable policy or procedure of the PCBU to comply with the WHS Act and WHS Regulation.

People with duties under the WHS Act should also refer to the Work Health and Safety Regulation 2011 (WHS Regulations) and Code of Practice.

WHS Regulation
WHS Regulation

    The WHS Regulation specifies the way in which a duty under the WHS Act must be performed and prescribes procedural or administrative matters to support the WHS Act.
Codes of Practice
Codes of Practice

    A code of practice provides practical guidance so that we can meet our health and safety duties. These codes give guidance on:

    • how to achieve the standards required under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (the Act)
    • effective ways to identify and manage risks.

    A code of practice applies to anyone who has a duty of care in the circumstances described in the code. In most cases, following an approved code of practice would achieve compliance with the health and safety duties in the Act, in relation to the subject matter of the code. Like regulations, codes of practice deal with particular issues and do not cover all hazards or risks which may arise. The health and safety duties require duty holders to consider all risks associated with work, not only those for which regulations and codes of practice exist. Refer to the WHS Amendments Fact Sheet – Codes of Practice for more information. Refer to the WorkSafe Qld internet page to view the current list of Codes of Practice.

Reasonably Practicable
Reasonably Practicable

    Reasonably practicable – the guiding principle of the WHS Act is that all people are given the highest level of health and safety protection from hazards arising from work, so far as is reasonably practicable. The term "reasonably practicable" means what could reasonably be done at a particular time to ensure health and safety measures were in place. In determining what is reasonably practicable, there is a requirement to weigh up all relevant matters including:

    The likelihood of a hazard or risk occurring (that is, the probability of a person being exposed to harm)
    the degree of harm that would result if the hazard or risk occurred (that is, the potential seriousness of injury or harm)
    what the person concerned knows, or ought to reasonably know, about the hazard or risk and ways of eliminating or minimising it
    the availability of suitable ways to eliminate or minimise the hazard or risk
    the cost of eliminating or minimising the hazard or risk.

    Ordinarily, cost will not be the key factor in determining what it is reasonable for a duty holder to do unless it can be shown to be "grossly disproportionate" to the risk. If the risk is particularly severe, a PCBU will need to demonstrate that costly safety measures are not reasonably practicable due to their expense and that other less costly measures could also effectively minimise the risk.

Safety Management System (SMS)Safety management involves managing your business activities in a systematic, coordinated way so that risk is minimised.

A safety management system (SMS) is a systematic approach to managing safety, including the necessary organisational structures, accountabilities, policies and procedures. A SMS allows the department to communicate a policy and objectives considering legislative requirements and information about hazards or risks. It applies to those hazards or risks over which the department may exert control and over which it can be expected to have an influence.
The implementation of the SMS helps to meet several business imperatives. Its primary purpose is to minimise the risk of injuries and accompanying costs. Other outcomes include compliance with legal requirements, meeting industrial relations expectations, accounting for ethical concerns and overall improved financial performance.

The TMR vision is "Connecting Queensland – delivering transport for prosperity" and TMR aims to achieve this through "An integrated, safety and efficient transport system for Queensland".

The creation of a positive high performing safety and wellbeing culture plays a vital role in achieving TMRs vision and is critical to improving the safety and wellbeing of TMR staff. It is equally important that robust systems across all levels of TMR are in place to support the Executive Leadership Team (ELT), Senior Management, employees and contractors in the day to day management of safety and wellbeing matters.

The TMR SMS promotes consultation throughout the workplace and acknowledges the importance of periodic review and evaluation to identify opportunities for continuous improvement.

Three (3) tiers of the TMR Safety Management System
Three (3) tiers of the TMR Safety Management System
    • Tier 1 – the top tier of the SMS sets out the WHS Policy and Workplace Rehabilitation Policy and relevant Frameworks, that clearly note TMRs commitment to ensuring safe and healthy workplaces and the responsibilities that we all have in achieving this goal.
    • Tier 2 – the second tier contains the WHS Procedures that assist us in achieving compliance with the relevant legislation
    • Tier 3 – the third tier contains WHS documents that assist TMR to comply with our policies, frameworks and procedures, including:
    • Forms, checklists, SWMS
    • Toolbox talks
    • Guidelines
    • Factsheets
    • Safety and Wellbeing SharePoint site

    See also the TMR Safety and Wellbeing Strategy 2016-2020

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)Personal protective equipment (PPE) is clothing, equipment or substances designed to be worn by someone to protect them from risks of injury or illness.

School Crossing Supervisors are to ensure that the minimum mandatory PPE is worn prior to entering and for the duration of the work being performed. Road Safety staff and any other TMR staff should also wear the relevant PPE when visiting / attending a school crossing site.

PPE does not control the hazard at the source. PPE should only be considered as a: -
    • Control measure when exposure to a risk can't be minimised in another way; or
    • Final barrier between the worker and the hazard when used in conjunction with other control measures
PPE provided to School Crossing Supervisors
PPE provided to School Crossing Supervisors
    PPE provided to School Crossing Supervisors includes: -
    • A high vis over vest – must be fastened at the front (to be worn under the raincoat jacket in adverse weather conditions)
    • Raincoat jacket and raincoat pants to be worn in adverse weather conditions (School Crossing Supervisor may elect to only wear the raincoat jacket)
    • Broad brimmed high vis hat
    • A hat cover for use in adverse weather conditions.
    • Tinted safety glasses / sunglasses. Tinted overlays for prescription glasses if required
    • Sunscreen – to be applied at least 30 minutes prior to the commencement of a shift and/or as per manufacturer's instructions
    • Cooling scarf (Chill-Its) where applicable to the Region

Incident Reporting and Management
Incident Reporting / ManagementAn incident is any event that occurs in the work environment, including the journey to and from work, which causes an injury or illness to people and/or damage to equipment or the environment. This includes accidents, aggression (which includes a variety of inappropriate behaviours directed at a worker in the course of them performing their duties), harassment or stolen property.

An incident is also an event that occurs at a workplace or is directly or indirectly caused by the department's undertaking, and results in the injury or illness of a person or damage to property.

A near miss incident is an event that occurs at a workplace, or is directly or indirectly caused by the department's undertaking and that the potential to result in the injury or illness of a person or damage to property.

A lost time injury (LTI) is an injury that results in at least one full shift being lost at their normal place of work, at some time (not necessarily immediately) after the shift during which the injury occurred, providing it is not a rostered day off. If the employee fails to report to their next scheduled work shift due to an injury or illness and provides a WorkCover medical certificate, it is a lost time injury.

Incident Reporting
Incident Reporting
    All workplace safety and security incidents, including near misses, must be reported to the manager/supervisor immediately. The incident must also be reported to the WHS Connect Hotline as soon as it is practical to do so.

    The importance of reporting Incidents is: -
    • To ensure that the appropriate corrective actions are undertaken
    • To assist the business understand why the incident happened
    • Assist with the prevention of future incidents of the same type

    Incidents are reported by calling the TMR WHS Connect hotline on 1800 867 947 (1800 TMR WHS) to easily report safety or security incidents. While there are other methods to report an incident, this is the easiest and preferred method.

    The WHS Connect hotline operates from 8am to 5pm Monday to Friday, however you can leave a voicemail message outside of these hours. If you use the out-of-hours voicemail, leave your contact number and the best time to reach you. All messages received out of hours are responded to within 4 working hours or the next business day.

    An incident notification will be emailed, which is to be checked for accuracy and any changes required are made.

Incident categories
Incident categories
    A high potential incident is an incident where any of the following has occurred: -
    A notifiable incident as defined by s35 of the WHS Act: -
    • The death of a person
    • A serious injury or illness of a person
    • A dangerous incident, this includes a serious electrical incident as defined in s11 of the Electrical Safety Act 2002
    • A dangerous electrical event as defined in s12 of the Electrical Safety Act 2002
    • A lost time injury (LTI)
    • The issue of an enforcement notice (prohibition notice, improvement notice) by WHSQ
    • A near miss incident where except through good fortune, any of the above would have occurred

    A medium potential incident is an incident where any of the following has occurred: -
    • A medical treatment injury resulting in the employee returning to work for the next shift, fully fit or on suitable duties (an LTI has been avoided)
    • A near miss incident where, except through good fortune, any of the above would have occurred

    A low potential incident is an incident where any of the following has occurred: -
    • Injuries resulting in first aid treatment only, no incapacity to work
    • A near miss incident where except through good fortune, any of the above would have occurred

    Low and medium potential classified incidents require a WHS Incident Corrective Action Plan to be completed. Refer to the WHS Incident Corrective Action Plan.

    High potential classified incidents require an incident investigation be undertaken and a report completed and approved by the General Manager within 15 working days of the date of occurrence of the incident. Refer to the WHS Procedure for Incident Investigations and the How to undertake an incident investigation guideline for assistance. Refer to the Incident Investigation Report form.

    Refer to the WHS Procedure for Reporting Workplace Incidents for more information.

Notifiable Events
Types of incidents that must also be reported to Workplace Health and Safety Queensland
Types of incidents that must also be reported to Workplace Health and Safety Queensland
    There are certain types of incidents that must also be reported to Workplace Health and Safety Queensland.
    A notifiable event is: -
    • The death of a person
    • A serious injury or illness of a person
    • A dangerous incident

    A serious injury or illness of a person is an injury or illness requiring the person to have: -
    • Immediate treatment as an in-patient in a hospital
    • Immediate treatment for any of the following: -
    • The amputation of any part of the body
    • A serious head injury
    • A serious eye injury
    • A serious burn
    • The separation of the skin from an underlying tissue
    • A spinal injury
    • The loss of bodily function
    • Serious lacerations
    • Medical treatment within 48 hours of exposure to a substance
    • Any other injury or illness prescribed under a regulation
A dangerous incident
A dangerous incident
    A dangerous incident is an incident in relation to a workplace that exposes a worker or any other person to a serious risk to their health or safety emanating from an immediate or imminent exposure to:
    • An uncontrolled escape, spillage or leakage of a substance
    • An uncontrolled implosion, explosion or fire
    • An uncontrolled escape of gas or steam
    • An uncontrolled escape of a pressurised substance
    • Electric shock
    • The fall or release from a height of any plant, substance or thing
    • The collapse, overturning, failure or malfunction of or damage to any plant that is required to be authorised for use in accordance with the regulations
    • The collapse or partial collapse of a structure
    • The collapse or failure of an excavation or of any shoring supporting an excavation
    • The inrush of water, mud or gas in workings, in an underground excavation or tunnel
    • The interruption of the main system of ventilation in an underground excavation or tunnel

    Any other event prescribed under a regulation
A serious electrical incident
A serious electrical incident
    A serious electrical incident is an incident involving electrical equipment, if in the incident a person:
    • Is killed by electricity
    • Receives a shock or injury from electricity, and is treated for the shock or injury by or under the supervision of a doctor
    • Receives a shock or injury from electricity at high voltage, whether or not the person is treated for the shock or injury by or under the supervision of a doctor

    A dangerous electrical event is any of the following: -
    • The coming into existence of circumstances in which a person is not electrically safe, if –
    • The circumstances involve high voltage electrical equipment
    • Despite the coming into existence of the circumstances, the person does not receive a shock or injury
    • The coming into existence of both of the following circumstances –
    • If a person had been at a particular place at a particular time, the person would not have been electrically safe
    • The person would not have been electrically safe because of circumstances involving high voltage electrical equipment
    • An event that involves electrical equipment and in which significant property damage is caused directly by electricity or originates from electricity
    • The performance of electrical work by a person not authorised under an electrical work licence to perform the work
    • The performance of electrical work by a person if, as a result of the performance of the work, a person or property is not electrically safe
    • The discovery by a licensed electrical worker of electrical equipment that has not been marked as required under this Electrical Safety Act.
How to report a notifiable event
How to report a notifiable event
    How to report a notifiable event – Workplace Health and Safety Queensland business hours are 8:30am to 4:45pm, Monday to Friday (excluding public holidays).
    You can report notifiable events by: -
    • Calling 1300 369 915
    • Completing and submitting the online incident notification form
    • Submitting a copy of the completed Incident Notification form to facsimile number (07) 3247 0297
    • Submitting a copy of the completed Incident Notification form via email to whsq.aaa@justice.qld.gov.au

    To report a notifiable event outside of business hours, call 1300 369 915 (press 1 at the prompt). This will connect you to the after-hours messaging service. Your notification details will be referred to an on-call inspector who will contact you within a timeframe that has regard to the seriousness of the incident.
    If you make a notification by telephone, written notification is not required.

WorkCoverThe Workers' Compensation and Rehabilitation Act 2003 establishes a workers' compensation scheme for Queensland, providing benefits relating to workplace injuries or fatalities, and insurance for employers.

A work-related injury is a personal injury arising out of, or in the course of, employment if the employment is a significant contributing factor to the injury.

Injuries can happen at work, travelling to and from work or while on a break from work. Injuries can also take place if a worker is travelling for work, or visiting other workplaces or sites for the purposes of their job.

If a school crossing supervisor has been injured at work, they should:
  • Seek medical assistance and get a work capacity certificate
  • Tell Road Safety Staff about the injury and provide them with the work capacity certificate as soon as possible
  • Lodge a claim, including the work capacity certificate with WorkCover Queensland
Lodging a Claim
Lodging a Claim
    Each case is reviewed based on the individual facts at the time.
    Once a claim has been made, it is allocated to a WorkCover Queensland claims representative to be determined.

    WorkCover determines claims as quickly as possible and will generally contact both the worker and employer within three business days of the claim being made.

    Workers can give details of the injury and event before the claim is decided. WorkCover will also speak to the employer to get further information (such as confirmation of employment and wage details) and to notify them that a new claim has been lodged on their policy.

    To lodge a WorkCover Queensland claim: -
    • Phone 1300 362 128
    • Complete and submit the online claim form
    • Complete a claim form and
    • Upload using the Workcover online service
    • Fax to 1300 651 387
    • Post to GOP Box 2459, Brisbane Qld 4001
    • Lodge through your doctor. If you're unsure if this has happened, you can either call Workcover or your GP to check.

Employee Assistance ProgramTMR provides an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) for all employees to provide free, short-term, confidential counselling services for both staff and immediate family members and to provide confidential manager support services for all department staff with supervisory responsibilities.

LifeWorks by Morneau Shepell (Lifeworks) have many counsellors located across Queensland in all major regional areas for staff wanting a face to face appointment. In smaller regional and more remote areas, LifeWorks can provide telephone or video counselling.

LifeWorks by Morneau Shepell is completely independent of TMR and no identifying information will be disclosed without written consent.

The EAP service is short term and solution based, focusing on resolving employee psychological issues with the help of a qualified professional counsellor. Counselling sessions can be for work or non-work related issues and do not require a referral.

People turn to counselling when they need support or are at a crossroads in their lives. Counselling gives them the chance to talk things through, very often relieving emotional pressure, clearing the way for them to focus and decide what to do next. Counselling can provide people with a sense of direction and a greater ability to achieve their goals.

TMR employees, have access to:
  • Confidential in person or phone counselling with experienced counsellors who can provide solutions to maintain or regain control of life
  • Online – web-based information resource for employees and immediate family
  • Support for management (information, coaching and guidance)
  • Critical incident services

Each TMR employee is entitled to four (4) counselling services annually. Each EAP session takes approximately 1 hour. The four (4) sessions can be used by either the employee or their immediate family members, or as group counselling.

Hazard Identification & Risk ManagementA hazard is anything that has the potential to harm people, property or the environment. At TMR, all hazards should be reported and recorded. Hazards can include substances, plant, work processes or the work environment. Before reporting hazards, ensure the safety of workers, if the hazard has the potential to cause immediate harm. If you can immediately control the hazard, then do so.

Everyone has a responsibility to identify and report hazards while at work. If you identify a hazard or witness an unsafe act or event that has the potential to result in an incident, it should be reported immediately to the manager/supervisor, then report is to the WHS Connect hotline 1800 867 947 (1800 TMR WHS). Refer to the WHS Procedure for Reporting Workplace Incidents for more information.

All injuries and illnesses are preventable. Reporting hazards helps reduce incidents and minimise potential risk to workers.

Risk management is an essential component in good workplace health and safety management. It allows you to control the safety aspects of your workplace activities. TMR's risk management process (risk assessment) consists of 4 elements. When these are used in sequence, we can continually improve our practices and reduce the risk of injury / illness to workers.

The 4 elements are: -
  • Identify hazards
  • Assess risks
  • Control risks
  • Review control measures
