Human Resources SCSS
About HR in TMRHuman Resource services and advice are an important resource in the effective management of the School Crossing Supervisor Scheme.

CSB road safety staff are required to become familiar with HR policies, issues and processes contained in this database and to seek early advice from their local HR Advisor on non-standard and/or more complex HR/industrial issues.

The role of a local HR Advisor is to partner with managers to achieve outcomes for the business. With respect to SCSS outcomes, HR Advisors can assist CSB road safety staff in many ways, including:
  • advising on performance management issues;
  • discussing forms and processes for addressing/resolving conditions of employment;
  • providing information on conditions of employment; and
  • delivering education/development programs on HR issues.

A key focus of HR professionals in Transport and Main Roads (TMR) is the importance of early intervention in management issues to avoid the escalation of issues such as poor performance and/or workplace conflict.

Identifying and addressing these issues early can save managers and supervisors considerable time and resources.

The HR team looks forward to assisting you to interpret and implement the contents of this database and work with your team to support best practice leadership and management within the School Crossing Supervisor Scheme.
Equity and DiversityThe Department is committed to a diverse and equitable workforce. A workforce that mirrors the community it serves is in a better position to provide services to meet the community's needs.

The diverse community of School Crossing Supervisors means that equity and diversity issues may arise in managing employment situations and enquiries. It is critical that your management practices incorporate the principles of equity and diversity.

The Department has developed strategies to ensure that we move towards employment equity in a planned way. You are strongly encouraged to familiarise yourselves with these strategies and incorporate the principles and practice of equity and diversity in all your dealings with staff.

Employment equity is about eliminating unlawful discrimination and creating a work environment in which all people can participate in employment opportunities and pursue careers effectively.

The emphasis on diversity recognises the broad range of people who make up the Australian workforce.

Ensuring equal employment opportunity and encouraging diversity is the responsibility of every person in the Department. All managers and supervisors have special responsibility for promoting, practising and implementing these principles.

inside HR - Enhancing Equity and Diversity section provides guidance for all employees to:
  • maintain a workforce free from discrimination, workplace bullying, sexual harassment and unlawful discrimination;
  • celebrate diversity; and
  • promote equitable access to development opportunities.

Relevant legislation, directives and Departmental policies and publications may be accessed from inside HR.

Entitlements
Pay
Pay
    Pay Rates
    Pay Rates
      • Rates of pay are determined in accordance with the General employees (Queensland Government Departments) and other employees Award – State 201 (refer inside HR)
      • Employees receive an hourly rate of the full time rate plus 23% loading to compensate for not receiving recreation leave or sick leave. Refer to s. 287 of the Industrial Relations Act 2016
      • Wages will be paid electronically into nominated bank accounts, and payment slips will be forwarded directly to the school crossing supervisor's address. PAYG payment summaries (group certificates) will be posted within 14 days of the start of each financial year.
      • Employment can be arranged for outside standard school crossing work hours when requested by a school or third party for a special event.
    Pay Increments
    Pay Increments

      Casual employees progress to the next increment level annually and once they have worked 1,200 ordinary hours at that level. In relation to progressing to the next increment, continuous service for a casual employee ends if the employment is broken by more than three months between the end of one employment contract and the start of the next employment contract. Absences from work on public holidays do not break, or contribute to a break, in the continuity of service.
    Superannuation
    Superannuation

      Superannuation is covered by existing government policy. Q-Super is the default superannuation fund for Queensland Government employees. For employees working small numbers of hours (such as school crossing supervisors) the department's contribution is calculated annually.

      If gross pay meets the minimum required for superannuation, a payment of 9.5 % of the gross base income will be made to a superannuation fund of the school crossing supervisors choice. However, if they do not nominate a superannuation fund the payment will be paid to the default scheme (Q-Super Accumulation Account) on their behalf. The school crossing supervisor may also elect to make personal contributions.

      For further information about superannuation, employees can contact the Advisory Service of the Government Superannuation Office on 1300 360 750.
Hours of Work
Hours of Work
    Hours of Employment
    Hours of Employment
      • School Crossing Supervisors are employed as casuals. As such they are engaged under the General employees (Queensland Government Departments) and other employees Award – State 2015 (refer inside HR) and are paid on an hourly basis to work less than the ordinary weekly working hours of a full time employee.
      • They will normally work for a maximum of 1.5 hours in any one day, Monday to Friday (excluding school and public holidays) which includes 30-60 minutes in the morning and 30 minutes in the afternoon – before and after school hours.
      • The minimum engagement for school crossing supervisors is 30 minutes
    Changes to Supervision Times
    Changes to Supervision Times

      Any changes to supervision times at a children’s crossing must be approved by the department in consultation with the school principal.
    Reduction in Hours of Supervision
    Reduction in Hours of Supervision

      If a crossing is closed or reduced in status from double to single operation, every attempt will be made by the CSB road safety staff to give the school crossing supervisor/s work in a similar position at a nearby crossing.

      However, if this is not possible, the department is not obligated in any way to ensure the continued employment of a school crossing supervisor.
    Working Arrangements
    Working Arrangements

      Each school crossing supervisor is required to work allocated weeks on duty/off duty for the whole school year for each children’s crossing as per the roster provided. School crossing supervisors are to be on duty at their rostered crossing for the entire approved period of operation for that crossing on any day students are present at school.

      The CSB road safety staff will advise a school crossing supervisor of the need to supervise a crossing on the student free day if it is utilised by both private and state school students who have differing student free days.

      A school crossing supervisor can be authorised by the Manager Road Safety and Senior Road Safety Advisor to use their own vehicle for official purposes

This is a standard endorsement available on request from all insurance companies. The policy does not require the actual words ‘Queensland Government’ the word ‘employer' will suffice.

      Refer inside HR for details of parental leave.

    Return from Leave
    Return from Leave

      Responsibilities for school crossing supervisors
      • Prior to commencing leave, a School Crossing Supervisor is responsible for confirming their expected date of return from leave.
      • School Crossing Supervisors are to report for duty on their expected date of return from leave. If they are prevented from doing so, they are responsible for advising CSB road safety staff and/or the school principal and explaining the reason for their inability to attend the crossing.
      • Where the School Crossing Supervisor has experienced ill health during the period of leave, they are to advise CSB road safety staff and may be required to produce a medical certificate from a duly qualified medical practitioner to confirm their ability to safely and satisfactorily perform the duties of the role.

      Responsibilities for CSB road safety staff
      • CSB road safety staff can take action to advertise a vacancy if a school crossing supervisor does not return to duty on the date stated in writing for a leave of absence and/or does not contact the road safety office regarding an extension of leave.
      • CSB road safety staff will retrain any school crossing supervisor returning from extended periods of leave, using the standard crossing drill or authorised version if the period of leave has exceeded six months.
      • Where the School Crossing Supervisor has experienced ill health during the period of leave, CSB road safety staff may ask them to produce a health assessment from a duly qualified medical practitioner to certify their healthiness to perform the duties of the role.
    Record Keeping
    Record Keeping

      • Keep a copy of all written requests for leave/forms and file on personnel file
      • forward original requests to regional pay section for processing
      • treat medical information confidentially and maintain records separate to the employee’s personal file

      Note:It is the responsibility of the Blue Card holder to provide this advice to the Commission.

      Forward a copy of the resignation advice to the regional pay office for processing and file the original on the school crossing supervisor’s personnel file.

      • Update the status on the database to show resigned.
      • If requested a reference regarding employment can be provided.
      • Retain the physical/hybrid personnel file container refer to local office procedures.
      • Archive personnel file container as per TMR Recordkeeping guidelines refer to Recordkeeping.
    Termination
    Termination
      Casual employees
      • School Crossing Supervisors are casual employees and so termination of their employment occurs through withdrawing them from rostered shifts.
      • Any decision to withdraw an employee from rostered arrangements at the school crossing is to be communicated to the employee with reasons for the decision.
      • Where this decision is taken as a result of performance, conduct or health issues, the Manager (Road Safety) and local HR Advisor are to be involved in the decision making process.

      Long term casual employees
      • The Queensland Industrial Relations Commission has determined that long term casual employees are eligible to make a claim of unfair or unlawful dismissal, because they are treated as similar to permanent employees in matters of termination of employment.
      • Where a decision is taken to commence a process of terminating a long term casual employee as a result of performance, conduct or health issues, the Manager (Road Safety) and local HR Advisor must be involved in the decision making process.

Grievance
Grievance

    The HR Reference Centre provides information and options about how to respond to complaints and grievances (refer inside HR) which have not been resolved through early intervention.

    Your local HR Manager or Advisor is to be included in discussions about complaints and/or grievances from school crossing supervisors or other members of the CSB CSB Road Safety staff to assist in developing strategies to resolve workplace issues.

    All Transport and Main Roads (TMR) employees, including school crossing supervisors, are eligible to submit a grievance if they have an honest belief based on reasonable grounds that:
    • an administrative decision made in their employing agency adversely affects them and is unfair or unreasonable; or
    • the conduct of an employee, agent or contractor of their employing agency adversely affects them and is unfair or unreasonable; or
    • the behaviour of an employee of their employing agency, whether by action or inaction, constitutes harassment or sexual harassment.

    An employee may not lodge a grievance unless they have made reasonable attempts to resolve the matter locally and informally. Exceptions to this are where the chief executive or their delegate determines that local resolution was not reasonable or appropriate.

    CSB Road Safety Staff and all members of the CSB Road Safety staff are to familiarise themselves with the current directive 02/17 Managing Employee Complaints and the department's policy on Grievance Resolution (refer inside HR) to be clear about the process and responsibilities for grievance resolution.

    Note:There are serious consequences for any proven victimisation of complainants – Refer: The Code of Conduct for the Queensland Public Service


    A school crossing supervisor is entitled to discuss grievance issues in relation to performance assessments by CSB Road Safety staff with:
    • Road Safety Staff;
    • Human Resource staff in each region;
    • Human Resource staff in the Executive Directorate, Transport Services Division;

Record Keeping and File Notes
Record Keeping and File Notes

    Record keeping is an essential requirement of managing performance and conduct issues in the workplace, but it must be done well and with consideration of relevant legislative provisions. Guidelines for writing file notes are included below for your assistance - your local HR Advisor can also assist as required.
    Writing File Notes:
    Writing File Notes:
        Purpose of a file note
        Purpose of a file note
        • Keep a documented paper trail of a process/issue;
        • Provide a documented resource for information needed in relation to any current or past process/issue; and
        • Provide reasons for decisions taken in relation to current or past workplace issues.
        The "Courier Mail" Test
        The "Courier Mail" Test
        The “Courier Mail” theory is based on the notion - never write anything in a file note that you would not like to see printed in the newspaper. In other words, do not include in file notes any information which is not relevant, appropriate or evidence-based. If unsure, check with your manager or HR Advisor.
        Language
        Language
        The language you use in a file note is very important because it can be viewed by:
        • The employee concerned; and/or
        • Senior management of Transport and Main Roads; and/or
        • A Court or Tribunal or Commission as evidence.
        Guidelines for Language
        Guidelines for Language
        • Write in plain English;
        • Always consider the “Courier Mail” theory;
        • Never write your opinions, only record your observations of someone’s reactions or actions; and
        • Never express or record your own emotions or feelings in relation to the reported behaviour – the file note is a record of fact and can be used in evidence of whether reasonable management action has been taken by the department – or yourself as a representative of the department.
        Structure and Content
        Structure and Content
        • When writing a file note, keep it simple;
        • Ensure you capture the fundamentals and that the information is accurate;
        • Capture the important components of the meeting/conversation/action i.e. purpose of communication, interaction and outcome; and
        • Make sure that you have dated the file note as per date of incident and ensure you include your name and signature at the end of the file note.
        Confidentiality
        Confidentiality
        • As with all departmental documents that relate to an employee issue or a departmental issue, it is vital that file notes are kept confidential;
        • All file notes are to be kept on a separate file that is accessible to relevant parties involved in the process/issue ONLY; and
        • Confidential file notes are not to be kept on personnel files.


    Important
    • File Notes are accessible by employees under Freedom of Information Act (Right to Information) and Public Service Regulation 2008.
    • Following these guidelines for effective file notes can prevent exposing yourself or the department to allegations of defamation and/or discrimination – sometimes the result of poorly written file notes.
    • Sign and date all file notes when completed.

Performance Management
Performance Management
    Performance and conduct
    Performance and conduct
      Managing the performance and conduct of school crossing supervisors is one of the most challenging responsibilities of the CSB CSB Road Safety staff, especially given the:
      • unique workplace environment in which school crossing supervisors work;
      • diversity of the crossing supervisor workforce;
      • size of the crossing supervisor workforce;
      • remote locations of some crossing supervisors;
      • reliance on school communities to advise of performance and conduct issues; and
      • serious consequences of performance issues relating to the crossing drill.

      The way in which performance feedback is provided to a school crossing supervisor or any employee can make the difference in their decision to make a complaint.

      Where a complaint is made by a school crossing supervisor in relation to a performance management process, investigation of the complaint will focus on whether reasonable management action was taken in respect to managing the performance or conduct issue.

      Where employees make a complaint or grievance, this will be dealt with in accordance with departmental policy and relevant directives and legislation (see inside HR - Managing Employee Complaints Procedure).
    Manage and monitor the peformance of new School Crossing Supervisors
    Manage and monitor the peformance of new School Crossing Supervisors
      CSB road safety staff will closely monitor the performance of newly appointed School Crossing Supervisors for the first 12 weeks of their engagement. A performance review will be conducted on every new school crossing supervisor by CSB road safety staff before the end of the first 12 weeks of employment.

      Performance assessments will be based on the standard crossing drill or authorised variations and should take place at the crossing where the school crossing supervisor normally operates and will include assessment of:
      • sound judgement of safe distances and speeds of approaching vehicles;
      • use of all personal protective equipment issued and safety equipment in a clean and serviceable condition;
      • operation of the crossing for the designated times for that crossing;
      • drill performed to standard with instructions being visible to motorists and pedestrians;
      • conduct at the crossing and interaction with school community; and
      • record keeping practices, including timesheets and incident reporting.

      This first performance assessment is also an opportunity to check understanding of matters raised at induction and for the crossing supervisor to clarify any issues or ask questions.

      The School Crossing Supervisor Performance Assessment form F1831 must be filed at the road safety office and processed onto the database.
    Ongoing performance monitoring and assessment
    Ongoing performance monitoring and assessment
      A formal performance assessment will be conducted for all school crossing supervisors at least once a year and/or at the discretion of the Manager (Road Safety) using the school Crossing Supervisor Performance Assessment form (F1831). The review of annual training materials and information sent throughout the year, relevant to the role of a school crossing supervisor, will form part of the annual performance assessment.

      Formal performance assessments will be conducted by the relevant CSB road safety staff or nominated officer which may include:
      • member of the school community;
      • police officer; and
      • other nominated officer trained in the crossing drill and departmental performance assessment processes.

      All nominated officers are to be trained in the school crossing drill and have experience in conducting performance assessments and delivering constructive performance feedback. Officers should ensure they have a copy of all annual training materials and information sent to the supervisor since the last annual assessment, to verify the supervisor has read and understood all required information.

      Performance assessments can also be conducted where reports are received about any performance or conduct issue. These assessments can include observation of the school crossing supervisor performing crossing drill without prior notice and will then be followed by discussions with the school crossing supervisor to give them an opportunity to respond to any reports or feedback.
    Sources of information for performance assessment
    Sources of information for performance assessment
      The unique work environment for school crossing supervisors means that their performance and conduct is subject to direct observation more often by members of the community than by representatives of Transport and Main Roads.

      Performance assessment of school crossing supervisors is therefore informed by both:
      • Direct observation by CSB road safety staff; and
      • Feedback or complaints from a member of the public, colleagues or school staff.

      Any reported performance issue or allegation of misconduct must be investigated by a nominated member of the CSB Road Safety staff - an opportunity given to the school crossing supervisor to respond to allegations and/or feedback. For more information please see Unsatisfactory performance; Complaints; or Discipline.
Unsatisfactory Performance
Unsatisfactory Performance

    The size and diversity of the school crossing supervisor workforce means that members of the CSB Road Safety staff will need to effectively manage unsatisfactory performance.

    CSB road safety staff are to refer to the department's People Performance Management Procedure (Refer inside HR) as a guide to how to address incidents or reports of unsatisfactory performance by a school crossing supervisor.

    The local HR Manager or Advisor can assist CSB road safety staff in how to apply the principles of performance management to the specific situation.

    Examples of unsatisfactory performance
    Examples of unsatisfactory performance
      A school crossing supervisor’s unsatisfactory performance or conduct may include:
      • a supervisor repeatedly arriving late at their appointed crossing;
      • not performing the crossing drill appropriately or correctly;
      • not performing their duties in a safe manner to the point of endangering the lives of pedestrians, themselves or other road users.
      • inappropriate communication with children and other road users;
      • breaches of the Code of Conduct for Queensland Public Servants; and
      • violent or abusive behaviour towards pedestrians and/or other road users.

      Depending on the nature of unsatisfactory performance and the reported reason for the performance issue, a member of the CSB Road Safety Staff may instigate performance management action after discussing with the crossing supervisor the reason/s for their unsatisfactory performance (Refer inside HR - Performance Management Policy).

      Ideally, the crossing supervisor will be actively involved in suggested solutions and agreed performance improvement actions.
    Performance feedback
    Performance feedback

      Performance feedback should be provided regularly to all school crossing supervisors to encourage two-way communication about workplace performance and ensure the early resolution of any issues.

      Information on providing performance feedback can be accessed from inside HR. All members of the CSB Road Safety Staff are strongly encouraged to access this information and also access practical training in how to deliver constructive performance feedback.

      By way of brief introduction to this information, constructive performance feedback must be:
      • relevant and reasonable;
      • regular, ongoing and specific
      • delivered as soon as possible after performance or conduct issues arise or are reported;
      • honest and impartial;
      • include suggested solutions and/or coaching for improvement; and
      • documented and kept in proper files.

      The quality of performance feedback and the way in which it is delivered can make the difference between performance management being experienced as a positive or negative experience.

      The way in which crossing supervisors are informed of suggested performance and conduct improvements can also impact on how well they perform the crossing drill and the ongoing safety of the school community.
    Principles of Natural Justice
    Principles of Natural Justice

      Natural Justice consists of, but is not limited to:
      • The employee having the opportunity to review all material that the delegate relies on to make a disciplinary decision.
      • The employee being provided with an opportunity to respond to the decision made by the delegate.

      The local HR Advisor can provide advice on how natural justice is applied to the disciplinary process.

      CSB road safety staff are encouraged to read the department's HR Policy and Procedure for Discipline (refer inside HR)
Performance and Health Issues
Performance and Health Issues
    Where it is suspected that the unsatisfactory performance or absence of a crossing supervisor is health related or where the crossing supervisor advises of a medical condition which may impact on them effectively performing part or all of their duties, the option exists to explore that medical condition, with the permission of the crossing supervisor.

    Where a member of the CSB Road Safety Staff identifies a deterioration in health of an existing crossing supervisor which is impacting on their ability to perform part or all of their duties, three options exist for addressing the situation.
    1. Informal discussions between the agency and the employee followed by consultation with the employee’s doctor (with the employee’s written permission). The department’s early intervention program can also assist with strategies to access information from allied medical practitioners (e.g. psychologists, occupational therapists) to address any limitations employees face in their work.
    2. Referral to Occupational Therapist (with the employee’s written permission and written referral from a medical practitioner) for functional capacity evaluation to determine physical capacity to perform the duties of the position and/or identify any limitations to effectively performing the role and the crossing drill.
    3. Where these approaches are not successful, the employee may be directed to attend a medical appointment in accordance with Part 7 of the Public Service Act 2008. This action is subject to:
      • The employee meeting the criteria of Part 7 of the Act, namely: “it is reasonably suspected that absence from duty or performance may be affected by a physical or mental illness or disability”; and
      • Agreement by the Manager (Road Safety), the local HR Advisor, and the HR Manager, Services Division

    Reference to the PSC Guidelinesof the Public Service Act 2008 is essential when contemplating this option. (see also Medical Referrals, inside HR)


    Note: Where it is suspected that health issues are impacting on performance, the HR Advisor and Manager (Road Safety) is to be advised and involved in the process.

They also have access to the local HR Advisor or Corporate HR Branch who can advise them of the process for grievance resolution Refer inside HR - Managing Employee Complaints Procedure
    Complaints and negative feedback regarding School Crossing Supervisors
    Complaints and negative feedback regarding School Crossing Supervisors
      • In the case of a complaint from the public about performance or conduct of a school crossing supervisor, the nominated member of the CSB Road Safety staff will request the complainant to detail their allegations.

    Points to Note:

    Grievance
    Grievance
      The HR Reference Centre provides information and options about how to respond to complaints and grievances (Refer inside HR) which have not been resolved through early intervention.

      Your local HR Manager or Advisor is to be included in discussions about complaints and/or grievances from school crossing supervisors or other members of the CSB Road Safety staff to assist in developing strategies to resolve workplace issues.

      All Transport and Main Roads (TMR) employees, including school crossing supervisors, are eligible to submit a grievance if they have an honest belief based on reasonable grounds that:
      • an administrative decision made in their employing agency adversely affects them and is unfair or unreasonable; or
      • the conduct of an employee, agent or contractor of their employing agency adversely affects them and is unfair or unreasonable; or
      • the behaviour of an employee of their employing agency, whether by action or inaction, constitutes harassment or sexual harassment.

      An employee may not lodge a grievance unless they have made reasonable attempts to resolve the matter locally and informally. Exceptions to this are where the chief executive or their delegate determines that local resolution was not reasonable or appropriate.

      CSB road safety staff and all members of the CSB Road Safety staff are to familiarise themselves with the current PSC 08/10 Managing Employee Complaints and the department's policy on Grievance Resolution refer inside HR to be clear about the process and responsibilities for grievance resolution.

      Note: There are serious consequences for any proven victimisation of complainants – see Code of Conduct for the Queensland Public service.

      A school crossing supervisor is entitled to discuss grievance issues in relation to performance assessments by CSB road safety staff with:
      • CSB road safety staff;
      • Human Resource staff in each region;
      • Human Resource staff in the Executive Directorate, CSSR;
      • Corporate HR Branch; or
      • Grievance Liaison Officer (Human Resources Branch)
      Recruitment and Selection decisions
      Recruitment and Selection decisions

        All TMR employed school crossing supervisors and CSB road safety staff may discuss any grievance regarding a recruitment decision with:
        • The chairperson of the selection panel
        • The local HR Manager or Advisor

        • The Anti-Discrimination Commission, Queensland, if their complaint alleges discrimination on any grounds. (see the Anti-Discrimination Act 1991)

        For assistance in providing effective feedback, refer to inside HR and/or your local HR Manager or Advisor.

Discipline
Discipline
    Discipline
    Discipline
      The department is committed to implementing fair and reasonable disciplinary processes where a problem exists with work performance or personal conduct and reasonable steps by the Department to improve the unsatisfactory work performance or unacceptable personal conduct have failed to result in a satisfactory performance or acceptable standard.

      In the event of disciplinary action the CSB road safety staff are to contact their local HR Manager or Advisor for advice on the correct procedure for the disciplinary process. The Manager (Road Safety) is to be advised at all stages.

      All employees are entitled to the principles of natural justice and these principles must be applied throughout the disciplinary process.

      Natural Justice consists of, but is not limited to:
      • The employee having the opportunity to review all material that the delegate relies on to make a disciplinary decision.
      • The employee being provided with an opportunity to respond to the decision made by the delegate.

      The local HR Advisor can provide advice on how natural justice is applied to the disciplinary process.

      CSB road safety staff are encouraged to read Transport and Main Roads' HR Policy and Procedure for Discipline (Refer inside HR).
    Outcomes of Discipline Process
    Outcomes of Discipline Process
      • School crossing supervisors are employed as casual employees and disciplinary action is subject to common law provisions, allowing for disciplinary action of an official warning or dismissal.
      • Where a disciplinary process has been undertaken and the outcome is dismissal, prior to any action being taken the Manager should consult with the CSB road safety staff and the local HR Advisor to ensure all departmental policies and procedures leading up to the termination and during the termination process have been adhered to and completed correctly.
      • Only the General Manager (CSSR) can approve a dismissal as a result of a discipline process. Where this does occur, CSB road safety staff are to treat the matter and all related records confidentially.
      • The local HR Manager or Advisor will provide information on how to manage the exit process for the school crossing supervisor.
      • As with any cessation of employment, all personal protective equipment is to be returned and the regional pay office notified.
Disclosure of Harm
Purpose
Purpose

    The purpose of this document is to:

    • Provide clear policy and procedures for the handling of disclosures, allegations and/or suspicions of harm in line with the Transport and Main Roads Risk Management Strategy for Roles with Child-Related Duties (Blue Card) 2010 which has been developed by the Workforce Policy Unit, Governance & Employee Relations Branch.
    • To ensure road safety staff respond as quickly as possible to an allegation or suspicion of harm to make sure the best interests of the child or young person is always the first priority
Scope
Scope

    This policy applies to School Crossing Supervisors (SCS) employed in regulated employment and who must hold a blue card in accordance with CCYPCG Act 2000. It includes:
    • Handling disclosures, allegations and/or suspicions of harm

    Out of Scope:
    • SCS policy and procedures for Incident Reporting (for example, traffic violations, workplace health and safety occurrences), outside the definition of disclosure of harm
Definitions
Definitions

    For the purpose of this policy, the following definition applies:

    Term
    Definition
    HarmAs defined under the Child Protection Act 1999 as “any detrimental effect of a significant nature on the child’s physical, psychological or emotional wellbeing. For harm to be significant, the detrimental effect on the child’s wellbeing must be substantial or serious, more then transitory and must be demonstrable in the child’s presentation, functioning or behaviour.

    Harm may be categorised in the following types:

    Physical abuse - for example, beating, shaking, burning, biting, causing bruises or fractures by inappropriate discipline, giving children alcohol, drugs or inappropriate medication.

    Emotional or psychological abuse - for example, constant yelling, insults, swearing, criticism, bullying, not giving children positive support and encouragement.

    Neglect - for example, not giving children sufficient food, clothing, enough sleep, hygiene, medical care, leaving children alone or children missing school.

    Sexual abuse or exploitation - for example, sexual jokes or touching, exposing children to sexual acts or pornography or having sexual intercourse with a child or young person under 16 years of age (even if the child appears to have consented).

    Disclosure of harmOccurs when someone, including a child, tells you about harm that has happened or is likely to happen.
    Suspicion of harmYou are concerned by significant changes in behaviours or the presence of new unexplained and suspicious injuries.
    Incident An ‘incident’ means any unplanned release of energy or event that occurs within the workplace and which causes (or has the potential to cause) an injury or illness to people and/or damage to equipment, buildings, plant, or the environment
    Regulated EmploymentEmployment of a type mentioned in schedule 1, part 1 (regulated employment); Working with Children (Risk Management and Screening) Act 2000
    This includes School Crossing Supervisors
    Child related dutiesS 156 Public Service Act

    (i) are to be performed at a place at which services are provided only or mainly to a child
    or children; or

    (ii) are to be performed in a role involving providing services only or mainly to a child or
    children; or

    (iii) involve contact with a child or children that is of a kind, or happens in a context, that
    may create an unacceptable level of risk for the child or children;

Background
Background

    Under the Working with Children (Risk Management and Screening) Act 2000 , employers in regulated employment must develop and implement strategies to identify and manage the risks to children in that particular service environment.

    It is the Department’s responsibility to implement risk management strategies to identify and minimise the risk of harm to children and young people in regulated employment.

    The TMR Risk Management Strategy for Roles with Child Related Duties 2010 requires relevant TMR divisions to develop and implement a Handling Disclosures, Allegations and/or Suspicions of Harm policy and procedures.

    TMR Corporate Human Resources currently does not have a Disclosure of Harm policy.
Policy Statement
Policy Statement

    In the event of a disclosure or suspicion of harm the SCS must report the incident as soon as practicable to CSB road safety staff.

    TMR staff are not to attempt to conduct an investigation or mediate an outcome between the parties involved.

    In the event that CSB Road Safety staff are unavailable, the incident must be reported directly to the Queensland Police Service (QPS). The SCS must still notify the road safety staff as soon as practicable.

    The road safety officer must document details required on the Disclosure of Harm Report and advise the Manager (Road Safety) that a Disclosure of Harm Report has been completed.

    In order to meet the Department’s obligation, the disclosure must be referred to the QPS either by telephone or in person on the same business day as reported (note: the Report must not be faxed to the QPS).

    Details of the Disclosure of Harm Report are confidential and must be filed in the employee’s personal record file only.

    A note of the date of disclosure must be recorded on the employee’s electronic record.
