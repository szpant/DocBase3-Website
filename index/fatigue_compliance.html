Fatigue Compliance (7452)

Fatigue Management
What it isDriver fatigue or drowsy driving is a safety hazard for the road transport industry. The main causes of fatigue are not enough sleep, driving at night (when you should be asleep) and working or being awake for a long time. It is important to be aware of the signs of fatigue.

The Heavy Vehicle National Law (HVNL) sets out obligations for all parties in the supply chain to help them in the execution of their obligation to manage driver fatigue, meeting chain of responsibility (COR) requirements.
Fatigue- regulated heavy vehicles
Fatigue- regulated heavy vehiclesNational heavy vehicle driver fatigue laws apply to fatigue-regulated heavy vehicles, which are:
  • a vehicle with a Gross Vehicle Mass (GVM) of over 12t
  • a combination when the total of the GVM is over 12t
  • buses with a GVM over 4.5t fitted to carry more than 12 adults (including the driver)
  • a truck, or a combination including a truck, with a GVM of over 12t with a machine or implement attached.
NHVR Work procedure
NHVR Work procedure
Work Diary Inspection
Work Diary Inspection
    The Department of Transport and Main Roads (TMR) has adopted the NHVR Work Procedures to promote the consistent interpretation and application of the HVNL across participating jurisdictions.

    The 'Driver Fatigue Work Procedure' provides guidance to Authorised Officers when conducting driver fatigue compliance checks. The work procedure contains instructions for both Written Work Diaries (WWDs) and Electronic Work Diaries (EWDs).
Drivers Base
Drivers Base
    Where a driver has two (2) or more employers, the driver may have a different base and records locations in relation to each employer.
Odometer Reading
Odometer Reading
    A driver is not required to enter location and odometer at the change, from a rest to a work change, if the driver has entered the location and odometer at the start of a rest break and neither the location or odometer reading has changed, so long as the rest break start and finish is recorded on the same page (that is, in the same 24 hour period).

    Odometer readings in a work diary can be used as evidence for a range of driver fatigue and/or a work diary offence and may assist in validating the accuracy of other work diary entries or indicate the need for further investigation.

    If the rest period starts and finishes on different daily sheets, the driver must record the location and odometer reading at the end of the rest break on the new daily sheet.
ExemptionsFatigue management exemptions (permits and notices) enable operators and drivers to apply for work and rest hours, work diary and record keeping exemptions if they cannot reasonably work under the legislated fatigue management requirements.

Fatigue management exemptions provide operators and drivers with the opportunity to use alternative methods to comply with the objectives of the Heavy Vehicle National Law (HVNL) while at the same time maintaining a high level of road safety.

Exemptions
Exemptions

    The National Heavy Vehicle Regulator (NHVR) has strict approval criteria for fatigue management exemption applications. Applicants must provide all supporting information requested when submitting their application.
      There are four main sections under the Heavy Vehicle National Law (Queensland) for Exemptions for divers of fatigue regulated heavy vehicles:
        1. Section 266 - allows for exemption by Commonwealth Gazette notice for a period of not more than three (3) years, a class of drivers of fatigue-regulated heavy vehicles to operate under the maximum work times and minimum rest times stated in the exemption.
        Example: National Livestock Welfare Work and Rest Exemption Notice

        2. Section 273 - allows for exemption by permit to allow for a period of not more than three (3) years a driver of a fatigue-regulated heavy vehicle, or a class of drivers of a fatigue-regulated heavy vehicle, to operate under the maximum work times and minimum rest times as stated in the exemption.

        3. Section 357- allows for the exemption by Commonwealth Gazette notice or permit for a period of not more than three (3) years, drivers of fatigue-regulated heavy vehicles to carry out the class of work to which the exempt applies.
        Example National Primary Production Work Diary Exemption Notice

        4. Sections 363 to 368 - allows for an exemption by permit for a driver with inadequate English literacy. The exemption can only be granted if the driver has a nominee to make the records and the records are of a standard equal to or greater than those standards required by a driver completing a work diary.

      The below national and state notices are located on the NHVR web site;
      • National Primary Production Work Diary Exemption Notice
      • National Livestock Welfare Work and Rest Exemption Notice
      • National Heavy Vehicle Work and Rest Hours Exemption (Personal Use) Notice
      • National Heavy Vehicle Queensland Electricity Entity Fatigue Record Keeping Exemption Notice
      • National Heavy Vehicle Queensland Electricity Entity Work and Rest Hours Exemption Notice
      • National Heavy Vehicle Queensland Electricity Entity Work Diary Exemption Notice

    NHVR - Notices and Permit Based Schemes
    Emergency Services
    Emergency Services
      In addition to the above drivers of fatigue-regulated heavy vehicles acting for an emergency service and who have time critical duties are exempt from work and rest hour requirements and from carrying and completing a work diary.

      Drivers acting for an emergency service and returning from attending an emergency are exempt from work and rest hour requirements and from carrying a work diary; providing non-compliance does not present an unreasonable danger to other road users.
    Sanctions
    When a contravention of the HVNL is detected, sanctions available to Authorised Officers include:
    • Formal Warnings for minor breaches;
    • Infringement Notices for minor or substantial breaches; and
    • A Brief to Adjudicator that may result in a Court Sanction for severe breaches or breaches for which Infringement Notices are not provided.
    Formal Warning
    Formal Warning
      An Authorised Officer may, by written notice, issue a Formal Warning if he/she reasonably believes:
        a. A person has contravened the HVNL; and
        b. The contravention is not substantial, severe or critical; and
        c. The person had taken reasonable steps to prevent the contravention and was unaware of the contravention; and
        d. The contravention may appropriately be dealt with by way of a warning.

      Note:The driver should be informed that the Formal Warning can be withdrawn by written notice (within 21 days) and a proceeding (Infringement Notice) may be taken against the person to whom the warning was given for the contravention for which the warning was given.

      Formal Warning Notices
    Penalty Infringement Notice
    Penalty Infringement Notice
      An infringement Notice may be issued as an alternative to court proceedings. An Authorised Officer who reasonably believes that a person has contravened the HVNL may serve the person with an Infringement Notice.

      Penalty Infringement Notice Guide
    Brief to Adjudicator
    Brief to Adjudicator
      An Authorised Officer who reasonably believes that a person has contravened the HVNL should issue a Brief to Adjudicator for breaches cannot be dealt with by way of a penalty infringement notice.

      A Brief to Adjudicator can be used to better reflect the seriousness of the contravention. A Brief to Adjudicator can also be used on occasions for repeat offenders or where the offence involves an appreciable risk of harm to public safety or the environment and serious risk of accelerated road wear, harm to road infrastructure, diminished public amenity and unfair commercial advantage.

      Brief to Adjudicator
    Direction/Prohibition
    Direction/Prohibition
      An Authorised Officer should only make a direction relating to driver fatigue if he/she believes on reasonable grounds that the driver has committed a fatigue related offence and either:
        a. The driver is impaired by fatigue that may have been caused by the occurrence of the offence;
        b. The commission of the offence occurred sufficiently recently that there is a risk that the driver may be impaired by fatigue;
        c. Where work and rest records are not available or cannot be relied on.
