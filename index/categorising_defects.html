Categorising Defects (Heavy and Light Vehicles) (7321)

PurposeThe purpose of this document is to provide a defect categorisation system that focuses on the safety risk posed by the continued use of a defective vehicle on the road.

It applies to both heavy and light vehicles.

Note:This document adopts the principles of the NHVR Defective Heavy Vehicle Safety Risk assessment (refer Reference Documents section)
Safety SystemsVehicles incorporate 4 broad categories of safety systems when properly maintained ensure the vehicle can operate safely on a road when operated within its design capacity.

These include but are not limited to:
Safety SystemsSubsystems
Vehicle Control
  • Steering
  • Suspension
  • Braking
  • Lighting
  • Wheel and tyres
  • Chassis
Occupant Protection
  • Occupant restraint
  • Occupant protection (cabin)
  • Body design and fitment
  • Windscreen and windows
Emission
  • Noise reduction
  • Fuel reservoir/system vapour
  • Engine exhaust emissions
General Safety
  • Vehicle design - projection, overhang, bumper bar pedestrian impact, front under-run protection
  • Lighting/horn
  • Speed limiting
Assessing Vehicle Safety RiskTo assess the impact of a compromised vehicles safety system will have on the safety of the vehicle the Authorised Officer should take into consideration:
  • The extent to which the component/s are compromised and its impact on the ability of the component to perform its intended function
  • The likelihood of the defective component/s further deteriorating and impacting the future performance and safety of the vehicle
  • Whether external factors will further compromise the ability of the defective component/s performing its intended function
  • The effect the defect/s the compromised system has on the continued safe-use of the vehicle

External Factors impacting safety
External Factors impacting safety
    The external factors that may impact the safe operation of a vehicle include but are not limited to:
    • Posted speed limits
    • Road features including winding and/or hilly roads
    • Traffic density
    • Weather
    • Lighting conditions
    • Nature of the load being carried
    • The drivers understanding of the nature and seriousness of the defects
Safety Risk LevelsThe safety risk a vehicle poses when used on a road can be categorised as either:
  • Imminent and serious where the safety risks are either:
    • Immediate, where the safety system has completely failed and it no longer performing its intended function. For example all lights inoperative at night.
    • Imminent, where the safety system has been compromised by a defective component and the Authorised Officer has a reasonable belief that the safety system will not be able to perform its intended function and may fail completely during the current journey. For example, the primary suspension component is severely cracked, where complete failure is imminent
  • Not imminent and serious, where the safety system has been compromised by a defective component and the Authorised Officer has a reasonable belief that the safety system can still perform its intended function (in a reduced capacity) for at least the duration of the current journey. For example a single wheel brake lining is worn past wear limits however the overall braking system performance still meets the required standard.
Defect Categories
Self-clearing
Self-clearing
    • 'Self-Clearing' is not to be used as a category for a Defect Notice. Defect Notice categories to be used are Minor, Major or Major (Grounded). The reason for this amendment is that the HVNL(Q) does not require an operator to return the a Defect Notice categorised as 'Self-Clearing'.
    • 'Self-Clearance' however can still be used as a clearance method were appropriate. For example: for a defect categorised in TRAILS/TICA as minor and the owner is only required to sign the self-clearance declaration and return the notice.
Minor
Minor
    Applies in cases where the Authorised Officer reasonably believes that the continued use of the vehicle does not pose an imminent and serious safety risk.

    When a minor defect notice is issued:
    • It may be issued for a period of usually 7 to 14 days but must not exceed 28 days
    • The use of the vehicle is allowed to continue during the specified time
    • The clearance method may be self clearance or AIS
    • The use of the vehicle must cease if the defect is not rectified within the specified time
    • A defective label may be attached if the Authorised Officer believes that the cumulative effect of a number of minor defects poses an immediate safety risk
Major
Major
    Applies where the Authorised Officer reasonably believes that the continued use of the vehicle does pose an imminent and serious safety risk. When a major defect notice is issued:
    • The defect notice must specify:
    • That the vehicle cannot be used on the road other than to move it to a stated location in a stated way
    • The time from which it cannot be used
    • Conditions stating "Vehicle may be driven to a place of repair. Vehicle may not otherwise be driven on road until relevant repairs have been undertaken".
    • The clearance method may be AIS or TMR.
    • A defective vehicle label must be attached
Major Grounded
Major Grounded
    Applies where the Authorised Officer reasonably believes that the continued use of the vehicle does poses a severe an imminent safety risk and the vehicle must not be driven from the inspection site.

    When a major grounded defect notice is issued:
    • A defective vehicle label must be attached
    • It is issued for a period not exceeding 28 days
    • The vehicle cannot be used on the road and must be towed or carried from the point of inspection
    • The clearance method should be TMR
