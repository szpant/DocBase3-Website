Criminal History Guidelines (4758)

Assessing suitability
TMR has broad discretion under various legislation to consider an applicant's criminal history, in determining whether they are suitable to hold an industry licence.

This policy is to be used as a guide only to assist decision makers in the exercise of their delegated function.

When considering a criminal history, decision makers should consider the following factors:
  • type of offence and relevance to the licence/accreditation
  • when the offences occurred (recent offences are more relevant than older offences)
  • any pattern of re-offending including the frequency of committing offences
  • the persons age at the time of the offences
  • type and severity of the penalty imposed (prison term, community order, fine)
  • degree to which the criminal history could adversely impact public confidence in the relevant scheme
  • any other relevant facts or circumstances.

A criminal history can indicate whether a person has an appropriate regard for and understanding of the law, and whether they may pose a risk to the safety of others they must interact with to perform their role and to the public and/or to property.

Particular care should be given to considering charges on a criminal history. It is important to remember that a charge is an allegation that has not yet been determined by a court and the person may be found not guilty. In assessing charges, decision makers should consider:
  • the type of offence and its relevance to the licence/accreditation
  • any pattern of re-offending, have they been convicted of a similar or related offence,
  • any other relevant facts or circumstances.

Charges cannot be considered for an Application for a Dangerous Goods Driver Licence.

A criminal history check must be conducted:
  • For all new applications
  • At renewal
      Note:New Zealand traffic and criminal history checks should be assessed using the same guidelines that are considered for Australian traffic and criminal history checks for Industry Authority applications.

Waiting period has passed
Waiting period has passed
    If the specified waiting period has passed before the application was lodged, the applicant should not generally be refused on the basis of that offence alone. However, they may be refused based on other relevant grounds such as their traffic history, whether they have previously had a licence or accreditation suspended or cancelled.

    If the specified waiting period has passed, you may consider any existing exceptional or special circumstances that may still justify refusing the application because of:
    • matters of ‘public interest’ and ‘public safety’ (applicable only for Traffic Controller Accreditation, Pilot & Escort Vehicle Driver Accreditation, and Driver & Rider Trainer Accreditation);
    or
    • the applicant is not an ‘appropriate person’ to hold the licence or certificate (applicable only to Tow Truck Licence holders, Tow Truck Driver / Assistant Certificate).
Waiting period has not passed
Waiting period has not passed
    If the specified waiting period has not passed before the application was lodged, the applicant ‘may potentially’ be refused on offence history grounds. In this instance, before refusing the application the following suggested factors (see below) should be taken into account.
Suggested factors in considering criminal history
Suggested factors in considering criminal history
    TMR officers must be fair and objective when considering criminal histories. Officers must balance the applicants ability to meet the eligibility requirements with the community’s expectation of the industry licensing role.

    Staff responsible for making decisions on industry authority applications and renewals must:
    • act in a fair and objective manner balancing the ability of the applicant to meet the eligibility requirements with TMR's and the community's expectation of the role.
    • consider the interaction required with others when performing the role such as police, emergency services personnel and vulnerable accident victims.
    Note: An industry licence should not generally be granted to a person who is still on parole (i.e. following a term of imprisonment).
Community Expectations
Community Expectations
    TMR and community expectations
    TMR and community expectations
      A person who is approved by TMR to hold a tow truck licence, driver and assistant accreditation are expected to be road safety conscious and have a high standard of conduct. In addition, tow truck drivers and assistants are expected to operate in an environment that is sometimes dangerous; near traumatised accident victims; and with police and emergency services personnel.

      TMR and the community place a high degree of trust and confidence in these licence and accreditation holders to perform their role in a lawful, professional and safe manner.
    What is expected of a driver/rider trainer
    What is expected of a driver/rider trainer
      Driver and rider trainers are expected to be responsible road users, to model the highest standards of driving/riding behaviour as they provide instructions to learner drivers (often young learner drivers). They are expected to conduct themselves in a responsible, respectful and professional manner.

    What is expected of a dangerous goods driver
    What is expected of a dangerous goods driver
      Dangerous goods drivers are expected to model high standards of safety and be responsible road users. They are responsible for transporting dangerous substances around the road network and are therefore expected to obey road signs, road rules and the ‘Australian Code for the Transport of Dangerous Goods by Road’ and have a high regard for human life, the environment and road transport infrastructure.

    What is expected of pilot and escort vehicle drivers
    What is expected of pilot and escort vehicle drivers
      Pilot and escort vehicle drivers are expected to model high standards of safety and be responsible road users. They are responsible for accompanying oversize vehicles and warning other motorists of an oversize vehicle approaching. They must interact with police, oversize vehicle drivers, other pilot and escort vehicle drivers and motorists, and are expected to obey road signs, road rules and specific requirements of permits and guidelines applying to accompanying oversize vehicles.

    What is expected of traffic controllers
    What is expected of traffic controllers
      Traffic controllers are expected to be safety conscious and act in a professional manner. They must interact with Police, motorists and road workers in dynamic and high risk road environments.
