Power of Attorney (2884)

What it isA Power of Attorney is the legal power to make decisions on someone else’s behalf.

This power is set out in a written document titled ‘General Power of Attorney’, ‘Enduring Power of Attorney’ or ‘Advance Health Directive’. The document is signed by the person (called the "principal") giving authority for another person (called the "attorney") to act on their behalf.

The document is required to be witnessed.

A General Power of Attorney can be witnessed by any adult.

An Enduring Power of Attorney and an Advance Health Directive also includes a witness’s certificate identifying the person who witnessed the principal signing the document. The witness must be a justice of the peace, a commissioner for declarations, lawyer or a public notary. The witness cannot be the attorney or a relation of the principal or the attorney.


    Note: A Power of Attorney cannot be used to apply for, or renew a driver, marine or industry licence.

Types of Power of Attorney
General Power of Attorney
What it isA General Power of Attorney, where stated in the Power of Attorney document, allows the attorney to make decisions (or sign documents) for the principal in their absence. A General Power of Attorney may operate for a specified period or a specific purpose.

Power of Attorney operates for a specified period
Power of Attorney operates for a specified period
    If the power of attorney operates for a specified period, as stated in the document, the power is revoked at the end of the stated time.
Power of Attorney operates for a specific purpose
Power of Attorney operates for a specific purpose
    If the power of attorney operates for a specific purpose, as stated in the document, the power is revoked when the specific purpose is achieved.
Proof of General Power of Attorney
Proof of General Power of Attorney
    The following documents may be presented as proof of a General Power of Attorney:
    • the original power of attorney document
    • a copy of the power of attorney document*
      *provided that:
      • each page, other than the last page, of the copy is certified to the effect that the copy is a true and complete copy of the corresponding page of the original power of attorney.
      • the last page of the copy is certified to the effect that the copy is a true and complete copy of the original power of attorney.
      • certification must be by one of the following persons:
        • the principal;
        • a justice of the peace;
        • a commissioner for declarations;
        • a notary public;
        • a lawyer (including a barrister, solicitor, barrister and solicitor or legal practitioner of the High Court or the Supreme Court of a State (including the Australian Capital Territory and the Northern Territory));
        • a trustee company under the Trustee Companies Act 1968;
        • a stockbroker.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s14
Who may be appointed
Who may be appointed under General Power of Attorney
Who may be appointed under General Power of Attorney
    The following may be appointed as attorney:
      • a person by name; or
      • a specified officer, or the holder of a specified office (the title of the office must be specified in the power or attorney document), for example the company secretary of ABC Company Pty Ltd.

      Note: If a specified officer or the holder of a specified office is appointed as an attorney:
      • the appointment of that officer or office is not revoked in the event the:
        • person who was the specified officer when the appointment was made ceases to be the officer, or
        • holder of the specified office when the appointment was made ceases to be the holder of the office; and
      • the power may be exercised by the person occupying or acting in the office concerned for the time being.

    A principal may:
    • appoint one attorney, or
    • appoint more than one attorney (joint or several*, or joint and several**, attorneys)

    *Where two or more attorneys for a matter are appointed and the power of attorney document does not state how they are to share the power given to them, they are appointed as joint attorneys.

    Where two or more attorneys for a matter are appointed and the power of attorney document:
    • specifies how each attorney must exercise power for the matter, and
    • that the attorney may only be held liable for the power for which they have been appointed to exercise
    the attorneys are appointed severally.
    **Where two or more attorneys for a matter are appointed and the power of attorney document:
    • specifies how each attorney must exercise power for the matter, and
    • both attorneys may be liable for the matter
    the attorneys are appointed joint and severally.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s13
Revoking a General Power of Attorney
Revoking a General Power of Attorney
Revoking a General Power of Attorney
    A General Power of Attorney may be revoked:
    • in writing by the principal
    • if a principal or attorney becomes a person who has impaired capacity*
    • if a principal or attorney dies
    • when the terms of the power of attorney have been met (i.e. specified period complete, specific purpose achieved)
    • if the attorney resigns
    • if an individual attorney becomes bankrupt or insolvent or takes advantage of the laws of bankruptcy as a debtor under the Bankruptcy Act 1966 or a similar law of a foreign jurisdiction (except for a power of attorney given as security (Refer: Powers of Attorney given as security below)
    • if a corporate attorney is:
      • wound up, or
      • dissolved, or
      • a receiver (other than a receiver for a limited purpose), or
      • administrator is appointed of the attorney.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, Chapter 2, Part 3

    *If a principal becomes incommunicate#, the court may, by order, confirm that, from the date of the order, all or part of the power of attorney remains in full force and effect if the court is satisfied that the confirmation is for the benefit of the principal.

    #A person becomes incommunicate if the person becomes incapable of communicating decisions about the person’s financial, property or legal affairs in some way.

    Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s18
Powers of Attorney given as security
Powers of Attorney given as security
Powers of Attorney given as security
    A power of attorney given as security is a power of attorney:
    • given by a principal as security for a proprietary interest of the attorney or the performance of an obligation owed to the attorney; and
    • that states it is irrevocable.

    A power of attorney given as security can only be revoked with the consent of the attorney while:
    • the attorney has the proprietary interest, or persons deriving title to the interest under the attorney have the proprietary interest, secured by the power of attorney; or
    • the performance of the obligation which is secured by the power of attorney, remains undischarged.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s10
Release of information for a General Power of AttorneyThe Department releases driver licensing information to the holder of a driver’s licence known as the “person” under section 77 of the Transport Operations (Road Use Management) Act 1995 (TORUM) or another person with the person’s written consent.

The attorney must therefore have the written consent of the principal (as the “person” under TORUM) to obtain the driver licensing information.
Enduring Power of Attorney and Advance Health Directive ("Enduring Document")
Enduring Power of Attorney
What it is
What it is
    An Enduring Power of Attorney authorises the attorney to make decisions on someone else's behalf, even if that person (the principal) loses the capacity to make decisions.

    A principal may, in an Enduring Power of Attorney, specify that the attorney is to:
    • make decisions (or sign documents) for the principal in their absence (in relation to financial matters only) and to continue to do so on the principal’s behalf after the principal loses their capacity to act for themselves
    and/or
    • make decisions (or sign documents) about the principal's personal or health matters after the principal loses their capacity to act for themselves.

      Example: The person has dementia.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998 s33

    Note:A "financial matter" for a principal, is a matter relating to the principal’s financial or property matters.
    A "personal matter" includes an application by the principal for a licence or permit.

    A "health matter" for a principal, is a matter relating to health care, other than special health care, of the principal.
    Powers of Attorney Act 1998

    A person dealing with the attorney may ask for evidence* to establish that the principal has the impaired capacity.
    Powers of Attorney Act 1998 s33(5)

    *Evidence may include but is not limited to, a medical certificate stating that the principal has the impaired capacity.

    An Enduring Power of Attorney giving power for a matter is not revoked by the principal becoming a person with impaired capacity for the matter.

    To sign documents on behalf of the principal in their absence (financial matters)
    To sign documents on behalf of the principal in their absence (financial matters)
      If the attorney has been given an enduring power of attorney to make decisions or sign documents on behalf of the principal in their absence for financial matters:
      • unless otherwise specified in the Power of Attorney document, the power begins immediately and the attorney may continue to sign documents on the principal’s behalf after the principal loses their capacity to act for themselves.

    To make decisions (or sign documents) about the principal’s personal or health matters
    To make decisions (or sign documents) about the principal’s personal or health matters
      If the attorney has been given the power to make decisions (or sign documents) about the principal’s personal or health matters:
      • the power to make those decisions does not begin until (if ever) the person is incapable of understanding the nature and foreseeing the effects of a decision, and of communicating that decision.
      • the power continues for as long as the person is incapable of understanding the nature and foreseeing the effects of a decision, and of communicating that decision.
Who may be appointed
Who may be appointed
    Only the following may be appointed as an attorney under an Enduring Power of Attorney document:
    • a person who is:
    • at least 18 years; and
    • not a paid carer or health provider for the principal; and
    • not a service provider for a residential service where the principal is a resident; and
    • if the person would be given power for a financial matter - not bankrupt or taking advantage of the laws of bankruptcy as a debtor under the Bankruptcy Act 1966 or a similar law of a foreign jurisdiction;
    or
    • the public trustee;
    or
    • a trustee company under the Trustee Companies Act 1968;
    or
    • for a personal matter only - the adult guardian.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s29(1)

    A principal may appoint one or more of the following:
    • a single attorney for a matter, or all matters;
    • different attorneys for different matters;
    • a person to act as an attorney for a matter, or all matters, in a circumstance stated in the enduring document;
    • alternative attorneys for a matter, or all matters, so power is given to a particular attorney only in a circumstance stated in the enduring document;
    • successive attorneys for a matter, or all matters, so power is given to a particular attorney only when power given to a previous attorney ends;
    • joint or several*, or joint and several**, attorneys for a matter or all matters;
    • two or more joint attorneys for a matter, or all matters (being a number less than the total number of attorneys for the matter, or all matters).

    *Where two or more attorneys for a matter are appointed and the power of attorney document does not state how they are to share the power given to them, they are appointed as joint attorneys.

    Where two or more attorneys for a matter are appointed and the power of attorney document:
    • specifies how each attorney must exercise power for the matter, and
    • that the attorney may only be held liable for the power for which they have been appointed to exercise
    the attorneys are appointed severally.
    **Where two or more attorneys for a matter are appointed and the power of attorney document:
    • specifies how each attorney must exercise power for the matter, and
    • both attorneys may be liable for the matter
    the attorneys are appointed joint and severally.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s43
Recognition of Enduring Power of Attorney made in other States
Recognition of Enduring Power of Attorney made in other States
    Where an Enduring Power of Attorney has been created in another state, as long as the power of attorney complies with the requirements set out by the state in which it was made, it may be considered as a valid power of attorney document in Queensland.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s34

    Note: A Power of Attorney cannot be used to apply for, or renew a driver, marine or industry licence.

Advance Health Directive
What it is
What it is
    An Advance Health Directive is an enduring document in which an adult principal may:
    • give directions about health matters and special health matters, for his or her future health care; and
    • give information about his or her directions; and
    • appoint one or more persons who are eligible attorneys to exercise power for a health matter (not including a special health matter) for the principal, in the event the directions prove inadequate; and
    • provide terms or information about exercising the power.

    The principal may give a direction by an Advance Health Directive:
    • consenting (in the circumstances specified) to particular future health care of the principal when necessary and despite objection by the principal when the health care is provided; and
    • requiring, in the circumstances specified, a life-sustaining measure to be withheld or withdrawn; and
    • authorising an attorney to physically restrain, move or manage the principal, or have the principal physically restrained, moved or managed, for the purpose of health care when necessary and despite objection by the principal when the restraint, movement or management is provided.

    A direction in an Advance Health Directive has priority over a general or specific power of attorney for health matters and is not revoked by the principal becoming a person with impaired capacity.

    An Advance Health Directive must be in writing and may be in the approved form.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s35
Who may be appointed
Who may be appointed
    Only the following may be appointed as an attorney under an Advance Health Directive:
    • a person who has capacity for the matter who is:
    • at least 18 years; and
    • not a paid carer, or health provider, for the principal;
    or
    • the public trustee;
    or
    • the adult guardian.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s29(2)
Operation of an Advance Health Directive
Operation of an Advance Health Directive
    A direction in an Advance Health Directive operates only while the principal has impaired capacity for the matter covered by the direction and is effective as if:
    • the principal gave the direction when decisions about the matter needed to be made; and
    • the principal then had capacity for the matter.

    An attorney’s power for a health matter under an Advance Health Directive is only exercisable when the principal has impaired capacity for the matter and not otherwise.

    While power of attorney for a health matter is exercisable under an Advance Health Directive, the directive gives the attorney for the matter power to do, for the principal, anything in relation to the matter that the principal could lawfully do if the principal had capacity for the matter.

    However, the power given is subject to the terms of the Advance Health Directive and the Powers of Attorney Act 1998.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s36

    Note:A person dealing with the attorney may ask for evidence* to establish that the principal has the impaired capacity.
    Powers of Attorney Act 1998 s36(6)

    *Evidence may include but is not limited to, a medical certificate stating that the principal has the impaired capacity.
Proof of Enduring Document
Proof of Enduring Power of Attorney and Advance Health Directive
Proof of Enduring Power of Attorney and Advance Health Directive
    The following documents may be presented as proof of an Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive ("Enduring Document"):
    • the original Enduring Document
    • a copy of the Enduring Document*
      *provided that:
      • each page, other than the last page, of the copy is certified to the effect that the copy is a true and complete copy of the corresponding page of the original Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive.
      • the last page of the copy must be certified to the effect that the copy is a true and complete copy of the original Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive.
      • certification must be by one of the following persons:
        • the principal;
        • a justice of the peace;
        • a commissioner for declarations;
        • a notary public;
        • a lawyer (including a barrister, solicitor, barrister and solicitor or legal practitioner of the High Court or the Supreme Court of a State (including the Australian Capital Territory and the Northern Territory));
        • a trustee company under the Trustee Companies Act 1968;
        • a stockbroker.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, s45
Revoking an Enduring Document
Revoking an Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive
Revoking an Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive
    An Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive (“Enduring Document”) may be revoked:
    • by the principal*
    • by an Enduring Document issued at a later date (a later Enduring Document)
    • if a principal or attorney dies
    • if a principal marries or enters into a civil partnership (unless otherwise stated in the enduring document)
    • if a principal divorces, the enduring document is revoked to the extent it gives power to the divorced spouse
    • if a principal's civil partnership is terminated, the enduring document is revoked to the extent it gives power to the principal's previous civil partner
    • when the terms of the power of attorney have been met (i.e. specified period complete, specific purpose achieved)
    • if the attorney resigns
    • for Enduring Powers of Attorney only, if an individual attorney becomes bankrupt or insolvent or takes advantage of the laws of bankruptcy as a debtor under the Bankruptcy Act 1966 or a similar law of a foreign jurisdiction (except for a power of attorney given as security (Refer: Powers of Attorney given as security below)
    • for Enduring Powers of Attorney only, if a corporate attorney is:
      • wound up, or
      • dissolved, or
      • a receiver (other than a receiver for a limited purpose), or
      • administrator is appointed of the attorney
    • if an attorney becomes a paid carer or health care provider for the principal
    • if the attorney becomes the service provider for a residential service where the principal is a resident

    *Enduring Power of Attorney
    The principal may only revoke an Enduring Power of Attorney in writing if they have the capacity necessary to make an Enduring Power of Attorney:
    • giving the same power; or
    • giving the same power for the health matter.
    *Advance Health Directive
    The principal may only revoke an Advance Health Directive:
    • to the extent it includes a direction about a health matter or special health matter,
    • to the extent that it gives power to an attorney

    and then only if the principal has:
    • the capacity necessary for the matter, or
    • the capacity necessary to make an Advance Health Directive giving the same power.
Powers of Attorney Act 1998, Chapter 3, Part 5
Release of information
Release of information for an Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive
Release of information for an Enduring Power of Attorney or Advance Health Directive
    An attorney appointed in an Enduring Document for personal or health matters has a right to all the information that the principal would have been entitled to if the principal had capacity, and that is necessary to make informed decisions about anything the attorney is authorised to do for the principal.

    Note:A person dealing with the attorney may ask for evidence* to establish that the principal has the impaired capacity.
    Powers of Attorney Act 1998 s36(6)
    *Evidence may include but is not limited to, a medical certificate stating that the principal has the impaired capacity.

    The Department releases driver licensing information to the holder of a driver’s licence known as the “person” under section 77 of the Transport Operations (Road Use Management) Act 1995 (TORUM) or another person with the person’s written consent.

    The attorney appointed under the Enduring Power of Attorney is the “person” under TORUM who can obtain licensing information.

    Where an Enduring Power of Attorney exists (e.g. Enduring Power of Attorney for financial matters) and the principal has capacity, the attorney must be authorised by the principal to obtain the principal's information.
