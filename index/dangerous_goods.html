Dangerous Goods (5177)

Statement
All goods carrying vehicles intercepted should be checked to determine if dangerous goods are being carried.

Inspectors should familiarise themselves with the Transport Operations (Road Use Management - Dangerous Goods) Regulation 2008 and the Australian Code for the Transport of Dangerous Goods by Road and Rail.

https://www.ntc.gov.au/codes-and-guidelines/australian-dangerous-goods-code

Dangerous goods must not be offered for transport unless:
  • Transport units have been appropriately marked and placarded;
    and
  • Transport units are otherwise in a condition for transport as required by the Code.

In addition the Code states:
'Packages must only be stowed and restrained in the transport unit in accordance with the Load Restraint Guide, or in a manner which provides at least the equivalent levels of safety and security'.

TMR considers that unless competent authority special requirements are met, the stowage of dangerous goods in the driver and/or passenger cabin areas, or stowed together with other freight and passenger luggage within compartments located directly below, or adjacent to, passenger seating - poses an unacceptable risk to those passengers in the event of an accident.

Accordingly, passenger coaches are considered as being not suitable for transporting dangerous goods (unless specially equipped - for example, a trailer) - therefore any person allowing such transport would be committing an offence. This could include the vehicle owner, supervisor, loader or driver who was aware of the carriage and allowed it to happen.

Therefore dangerous goods must not be transported by passenger carrying buses/coaches in Queensland - unless specifically permitted in writing by TMR.

For the purposes of the requirement dangerous goods does not include:
  • Internal combustion engines fitted to machinery - providing it is free of fuel and the tank cap is firmly in place;
  • Small containers carried by a driver or passenger for personal use (for example, aerosol and perfumery products);
  • Any device required to be carried for use in a fire or for medical needs (for example, fire extinguisher and oxy-reviver kit);
    and
  • Diagnostic specimens sent for pathology testing when not known to be infectious substances.

Note:The transportation of designated explosives of Class 5.1 (e.g. Ammonium Nitrate and Ammonium Nitrate Emulsions) are under the responsibility of the Explosives Inspectorate within the Department of Mines and Energy.
Procedure
The following procedure is to be followed in accordance with dangerous goods carrying practices and instruction:
  • Check for obvious signs as follows:
    • class labels on packages,
    • UN number etc. on documentation,
    • follow on-road interception procedures.
  • Where an offence is suspected, a Penalty Infringement Notice or 'Dangerous Goods' Interception Report (F1859) form as applicable, is to be completed and submitted to the Senior Inspector.
  • The dangerous goods interception report is available on the corporate forms database and can also be found below under section 'Dangerous goods interception report'.
  • See the PIN Codes list for a list of relevant PIN Codes.
Note: Under the Transport Operations (Road Use Management - Dangerous Goods) Regulation 2008, where a dangerous goods vehicle needs to be towed; the prime contractor must ensure that the driver of the tow truck towing the vehicle carrying dangerous goods either holds a dangerous goods driver licence; or is accompanied, in the cabin of the tow truck, by a person who holds a dangerous goods driver licence.
Procedures
Directions given due to loading
Directions given due to loading
    It is necessary at times for inspectors to give verbal directions to vehicle operators transporting goods, to alter or adjust their loading in the interests of public safety.

    Legislation provides for an Authorised Officer to give such directions as may be necessary to ensure the safe carriage of dangerous goods in accordance with the Act.

    The following procedure is to be adopted when inspectors find it necessary to give a direction for the vehicle to be moved to comply with the Dangerous Goods Code and Regulations:
    • Give a verbal direction that the vehicle is to be taken to a specific location, in accordance with the Act.
    • Whenever a direction is given, details are to be recorded on the Brief to Adjudicator. The results of such directions must also be recorded.

    Note: Under no circumstances are inspectors to escort any vehicle from point of interception to any other location. It is the responsibility of the driver to comply with any direction given by an authorised officer.

    Note: If an escort is necessary, for whatever reason, the Police should be advised and their assistance sought. For example, in the case of an uncooperative driver.
Attending vehicle incidents/spillage
Attending vehicle incidents/spillage
    Inspectors may be asked to attend dangerous situations involving dangerous goods vehicles to investigate and establish any possible breach of the Regulation in accordance with the Dangerous Goods Code.

    Although inspectors may be required to attend these incidents it should be noted that they are not part of any emergency service group and play no role in the function of these services; unless requested to assist, by a member of the police or emergency services personnel.

    The following procedures are to be strictly observed when inspectors attend the scene of an incident involving a dangerous goods situation.

    1.Upon arrival at a scene inspectors must observe all instructions given by emergency service personnel in attendance. If inspectors are first on scene, approach the area with extreme caution from an upwind position making observations to assist in the identification of the product/s involved. Ensure that emergency services have been notified and advise others to keep away.
    2.Identify themselves to emergency services, usually the Site Commander (Police) or Control Point (Fire Services) at the scene and explain the purpose and reason for being there; offer any assistance (with their competencies) that may be required and follow their instructions.
    3.make preliminary enquiries to police to establish the name and rank of the officer in charge of the site and give regular updates (where possible and time permits) to the STI, including such information as; road closures and other information.
    4.Upon emergency services declaring the area safe, inspectors may then approach the vehicle/s loading or office in charge and seek further information relevant to the incident.
    5.Obtain all relevant documentation pertaining to the loading, vehicle/s particulars, driver/s and the owner/s particulars, including all emergency procedure guides to establish compliance with the Code.
    6.Photograph the scene, the vehicle/s involved, and the loading. Establish whether further assistance such as technical advice is required from the Dangerous Goods Unit.
    7.Make note of the site of the leakage, with particular regards to tankers which may be leaking from component failure involving a hatch cover seal or a valve located in the cover. This will assist the Dangerous Goods Unit in determining if a revision of an Australian Standard is required. This would equally apply to drums and IBC's etc.
    8.Submit a report on the incident to your Senior Inspector within 24 hours.

    Note:Inspectors must at all times be aware of the relevant sections of the Work Place Health and Safety Act and Regulations pertaining to dangerous substances and refrain from placing themselves and other persons in a hazardous situation.

    Note:When proceeding to an incident or spill, inspectors shall abide by all road rule requirements.

    Note: A dangerous situation means a situation involving the transportation of dangerous goods by road that is causing or is likely to cause imminent risk of:
    • Death of, or significant injury to, a person; or
    • Significant harm to the environment; or
    • Significant damage to property.
Use of battery operated equipment
Use of battery operated equipment
    This instruction relates to concerns about the use of 'mag-lite' torches and other battery operated equipment, when inspecting dangerous goods vehicles. The purpose of the 'mag-lite' torch, used by compliance staff, is for inspections such as: correct placarding/marking, labels, number plates, axle groups, checking paperwork and work diaries. During these types of inspections there should not be any movement of product.

    In addition to 'mag-lite' torches, inspectors use other battery operated equipment such as mobile phones, tape recorders, cameras etc.

    When a dangerous goods vehicle carrying classes 22.1, 3, 4 or 5.2 is intercepted officers should:
    • Keep battery operate equipment that is, tape recorders, mobile phones, cameras etc., at least two (2) metres away from any openings/fittings that are used for filing and/or transfer of the product;
    • Not change or remove batteries in any equipment including the 'mag-lite' torch within eight (8) metres of the openings/fittings that are used for filling and/or transfer of the product;
      and
    • Not use mobile phones (including hand held radios) within 15 metres of the openings/fittings that are used for filling and/or transfer of product.

    If a leak is detected while inspecting a vehicle carrying either class 2.1, 3, 4 or 5.2 the inspector should take the following actions:
    • Turn off any/all battery operated equipment and advise the driver so that he/she can take the appropriate action in relation to a 'dangerous situation'.
    • Inspectors should move upwind of the vehicle and then approach the vehicle from an upwind position. Ensure that emergency services have been notified and advise others to stay away.
    • On arrival of emergency services explain your attendance offering any assistance that may be required. Inspectors are not part of any emergency services group and play no role in the function of these services, unless requested to assist by a member of the police or fire services.
    • Make preliminary enquiries to police to establish the name and rank of the officer in charge of the operation and give regular updates to the STI.
    • Upon emergency services declaring the area safe, inspectors may then approach the officer in charge and seek further information relevant to the incident.
    • Obtain all relevant documentation pertaining to the load, vehicle particulars, driver and owner details and all emergency procedure guides to establish any breach of the Code.
    • Photograph the scene, the vehicle and the load. Establish whether further assistance of the Advisor (Dangerous Goods) is required.
    • Make note of the site of the leakage, with particular regards to tankers which may be leaking from a component failure involving a hatch cover seal or a valve located in the cover. This will assist the Advisor (Dangerous Goods) in determining if a revision of an Australian Standard is required. This would also apply to drums, IBC's etc.
    • Submit a report on the incident to your Senior Inspector within 24 hours.

Minimum personal protective and safety equipment on road vehicles transporting a placard load
Minimum personal protective and safety equipment on road vehicles transporting a placard load
    The attached table specifies the minimum personal protective and safety equipment that must be provided, based on the classification of the dangerous goods being transported.

    A road vehicle transporting a placard load of dangerous goods must carry the personal protective equipment and safety equipment specified in the table for all the dangerous goods in the load, based on their primary hazards and any subsidiary risks, subject to any conditions incorporated in the table and its explanatory notes.

    All personal protective equipment and safety equipment provided in accordance with these requirements must be:
    • Clean;
      and
    • Suitable for purpose;
      and
    • In sound operating condition, ready for use.

    Personal protective equipment provided in accordance with these requirements must be in sufficient quantities for and suitable for use by:
    • The driver of the vehicle;
      and
    • Where required for escape purposes, any other person travelling in the vehicle.

    Respiratory protection equipment required to be carried for escape purposes must be carried securely and in an accessible position in the cabin of the vehicle.

    Other personal protective equipment provided for occupants of a road vehicle transporting dangerous goods must be carried securely and in a readily accessible position in the vehicle.

Dangerous goods classifications
Dangerous goods classifications
    Dangerous goods are classified (some of these classes are subdivided into divisions) and listed in the Australian Code of the Transport of Dangerous Goods by Road and Rail.

    ClassDivisionDescription
    Class 1: Explosives1.1Substances and articles which have a mass explosion hazard
    1.2Substances and articles which have a projection hazard but not a mass explosion hazard
    1.3Substances and articles which have a fire hazard and either a minor blast hazard or a minor projection hazard or both, but not a mass explosion hazard
    1.4Substances and articles which present no significant hazard
    1.5Very insensitive substances which have a mass explosion hazard
    1.6Extremely insensitive articles which do not have a mass explosion hazard
    Class 2: Gases2.1Flammable gases

    4.3Substances which in contact with water emit flammable gases
