Medical Condition Reporting Guide (1428)


What it isQueensland licence holders have a legal responsibility to notify TMR if they:
  • have a long-term or permanent medical condition that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive safely;
    or
  • have an increase in, or other aggravation to, an existing long-term or permanent medical condition that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive safely.

Licence holders must notify TMR as soon as they become aware of the development of, or adverse change to an existing medical condition. They must not wait until they renew their licence.
Medical fitness to drive
Assessing fitness to drive
Assessing fitness to drive
    An applicant for, or the holder of a Queensland driver licence must be medically fit to drive. The person’s treating health professional is responsible for assessing their medical fitness to drive.

    The health professional will conduct their assessment in accordance with standards provided in the Assessing Fitness to Drive for commercial and private vehicle drivers (AFTD) publication.

    The AFTD is a national publication designed to guide and support health professionals in providing an opinion regarding a person’s medical fitness to drive, and driver licensing authorities in making licensing decisions.

    The publication is publicly available online at -     http://www.austroads.com.au/drivers-vehicles/assessing-fitness-to-drive

Notifying TMR and actions to be takenWhen TMR is notified of a licence holder’s medical condition, action must be taken within one business day from when the advice is received.

In certain circumstances, TMR may take action to immediately amend, or suspend a licence.

Note:When a notification has been received, TICA should be checked to determine if the person is also the holder of an industry authority. If they are, Refer: Industry Authority > 1. Industry Licences > MCR (IA Holders) for instructions.

Notification by licence holder
Notification by licence holder
    All Queensland licence holders have a legal responsibility to notify TMR of any permanent or long-term medical condition, or any increase in or aggravation of an existing medical condition, that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive safely.

    To meet the legal requirements for notification the licence holder must provide written notice in one of the following approved forms:
    • Medical Condition Notification (F4355) and/or
    • Medical Certificate for Motor Vehicle Driver (F3712), or
    • Online Medical Condition Notification, or
    • any other form of written notice from the customer or a health professional (at the Manager's discretion)

    The Chief Executive may also take action based on written or verbal information received from the licence holder, with or without supporting information from a health professional, if the information indicates the person has:
    • a permanent or long-term medical condition that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive safely;
      or
    • an increase in, or other aggravation to, an existing long-term or permanent medical condition that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive.

      Verbal notification
      Verbal notification
        If a customer verbally indicates that they have:
        • a permanent or long-term medical condition that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive safely;
        or
        • an increase in, or other aggravation to, an existing long-term or permanent medical condition that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive

        they are required to complete the Medical Condition Notification (F4355). If the customer is unable to provide a Medical Certificate, the Medical Condition Notification is emailed to the MCR Unit.

        If a customer refuses to complete the Medical Condition Notification (F4355), the operator is to email the MCR Unit a 'record of customer interaction' advising that the customer had verbally advised that they had a medical condition that would affect their ability to drive. The 'record of customer interaction' must include:
        • Operator's name
        • Centre
        • Contact number for the CSC
        • Date
        • Reason for attending CSC (ie licence renewal, etc)
        • Details of the disclosure

        If a Licence renewal is due then a NOLR restriction must be added by the operator. A text restriction must also be added, which must state that a medical condition has been disclosed by the customer. The text must include details on the disclosure, that relevant documentation has been emailed to the MCR Unit for Show Cause action, Userid, CSC, Date and contact number.
    Notification by health professional
    Notification by health professional
      Health professionals may advise TMR directly if they believe their patient is not medically fit to drive, or their licence should be subject to conditions and/or periodic review. However, it is not mandatory for health professionals to notify TMR. Indemnity against liability is provided for health professionals who provide information in good faith about a person’s medical fitness to drive.

      A health professional means one of the following:
      • a doctor (that is, a medical practitioner);
        or
      • a person registered under the Occupational Therapists Registration Act 2001 or a corresponding law to that Act;
        or
      • a person registered under the Health Practitioner Regulation National Law to practise in the optometry profession, other than as a student;
        or
      • a person registered under the Health Practitioner Regulation National Law to practise in the physiotherapy profession, other than as a student.

      Health professionals may notify TMR about their patient’s medical fitness to drive via:
      • Online
      • Email to the MCR Unit
      • Written advice on official letterhead, or with an office stamp
      • Medical Condition Notification (F4842)
      • Medical Condition Notification form from the Assessing Fitness to Drive publication
      Note:It is not compulsory for health professionals to notify TMR, however they are encouraged to do so if they believe their patient will not notify TMR about their medical condition, or their patient will not comply with their advice regarding driving and/or recommended treatment.

    Notification by Queensland Police Service (QPS) or interstate authorities
    Notification by Queensland Police Service (QPS) or interstate authorities
      The QPS through their enforcement of transport legislation and/or attendance at police incidents may notify TMR about a licence holder's medical fitness to drive. Notification must be in writing, or a copy of the policy incident report provided. TMR will take action based on the information/evidence provided by the QPS where it is evident from the information provided that there is a reasonable likelihood that the person has a permanent or long-term medical conditional that is likely to adversely affect their ability to drive safely.

      Where TMR decides not to commence show cause action on the basis of advice from the QPS, the officer requesting the action should be advised that police are delegated under section 125 of the Transport Operations (Road Use Management - Driver Licensing) Regulation 2010 to give a person a written show cause notice proposing the amendment, suspension or cancellation of their driver licence. That is, QPS may like to initiate the show cause action themselves.

      Where the QPS have notified TMR about a licence holder's medical fitness to drive, a ground exists to show cause the licence holder. There is no requirement to acknowledge the incoming correspondence using the Third Party Acknowledgement letter, unless the QPS have requested acknowledgement.

    Notification by third party
    Notification by third party
      Relative, neighbour, friend or member of the public
      Relative, neighbour, friend or member of the public
        A relative, neighbour, friend of the licence holder or member of the public may notify TMR about a licence holder's medical fitness to drive. Action must not be taken unless the person has provided information and based on the weight of that information, it is reasonable to commence show cause action to amend, or cancel the person’s driver licence. Consideration should also be given to whether the person has a current medical certificate and if so, when the medical certificate was issued and when it expires.

        Third party notifications and the information provided is considered on a case by case basis. Anonymous notifications will not be actioned.
        Examples
        Examples
          An example of when it is reasonable for TMR to commence show cause action:

          TMR has received written advice from a driver licence holder’s family member advising that the driver licence holder has a permanent, or long-term medical condition and as a result they have been admitted to hospital on a number of occasions over the last few months. The person has also recently been involved in a crash where their medical condition was a contributing factor;
          and
          a TICA check indicates the person’s medical certificate will expire in one month;
          or
          a TICA check indicates the person’s medical certificate expired two months ago.

          An example of when it is unreasonable for TMR to commence show cause action:

          TMR has received written advice from a driver licence holder’s neighbour stating that in their opinion the person is not medically fit to drive. But the neighbour has not provided any evidence to support their claims that the person has a permanent or long-term medical condition. On checking TICA, it is identified that the person has a current medical certificate, which has only just been issued and has a validity of 12 months. It is not reasonable for TMR to commence show cause action on medical grounds because the person has recently been assessed by their treating health professional as medically fit to drive and the health professional does not require review for 12 months.


        All Third party notifications must be emailed to the MCR Unit. The MCR Unit will issue the third party acknowledgement letter and take any necessary action.

    Expired Driver Licence
    Expired Driver Licence
      If a customer’s driver licence is expired and notification is received about a permanent or long term medical condition (and the customer has not provided a current medical certificate), the customer must be advised in writing of the following:
      • That they must provide a current medical certificate (recommending they are medically fit to drive) before their licence can be renewed either in person or online.
      • That a restriction has been placed on their record to prevent them from renewing in person or online. The restriction also prevents them from using any online services for 28 days. Once the 28 days has expired or the medical certificate is provided (whichever is earlier), the online restriction will be lifted.
      Note: The NOAC restriction can't be applied if the customer is enrolled in Direct Debit as this will stop their registration from being renewed.

      Restrictions
      Restrictions
        • CSC operators must contact the MCR unit from them to place a NOAC and a text restriction (TMR APPLIED) on the customer's record (“customer must provide a current medical certificate. If the customer does not provide a current medical certificate, their application should be refused”).
        • The NOAC is only required for 28 days after the driver licence expiry date as that is the time frame when a customer can renew a licence online. After the 28 days has passed, the MCR unit will expire the NOAC to ensure the customer can use other online services.
        • MCR operators should create a manual follow-up reminder to ensure the NOAC is lifted after the 28 days. If the customer provides a medical certificate prior to the 28 days, the NOAC can removed and they can renew online.
        • The MCR Unit should complete the below "MCR - Expired Driver Licence Letter" and post it to the customer.
        • The text restriction must remain in place until the customer provides a medical certificate recommending they are medically fit to drive.

      MCR - Expired Driver Licence Letter
