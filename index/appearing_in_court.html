Appearing in Court (2770)


What it isThe department of Transport and Main Roads (TMR) staff can be requested to give evidence or produce documents in Court for criminal and civil matters.
Who can Make a RequestA request to appear in Court can be made by:
RequesteeDetails
TMR Prosecutions UnitYou may receive a phone call from TMR Prosecutions to discuss the issues and to determine if you may be required to go to court.

Travel arrangements are arranged through discussions with Prosecution Unit. All other expenses (eg. meals) can be claimed under normal departmental process.
QPS/Civil ProsecutionsIf the matter is being prosecuted by QPS or is a civil matter you may be sent a Subpoena or Summons for a Witness.

You will also receive a cheque for witness expenses (eg travel) which should be kept. After court contact the TMR Finance Unit for the money to be banked.
SubpoenaA subpoena directs TMR staff to give evidence and/or produce original or certified documents.

You should immediately contact TMR Legal Services if you receive a Subpoena to appear in court to give evidence about:
  • a matter relating to a decision or action you have taken in your duties as an employee of the Department; and/or
  • the content of a court certificate/court document you have prepared; and/or
  • TMR policy or procedural information.

Legal Services keep a central database of subpoenas served on the department and can discuss the subpoena with you and provide advice and assistance as required.

Upon receipt of a subpoena, you may ask the requesting officer whether you can provide a court certificate or appear via phone, in place of appearing in person. Please note, however, the final decision about whether you are required to appear in person is made by the magistrate or judge. Please ensure you advise Legal Services of the response you receive from the requesting officer when you contact them.

Note: If you receive a subpoena to give evidence about the content of a court certificate/document, confirm with the requesting officer whether you will be asked to provide evidence outside of what is covered in the certificate/document.

In the event the requesting officer confirms that you could be required to provide additional evidence, advise them that additional time may be required, as arrangements may need to be made for another TMR staff member to attend court in your place to provide this evidence.

Following the receipt of a subpoena, immediately contact Legal Services to advise them of the subpoena and that you have received confirmation that additional evidence may be required.

Legal Services will then engage with the Police Prosecutor and, if required, another TMR staff member will be nominated to attend court in your place.

If you are required to attend court, the Police Prosecutor will make contact with you prior to the court date to brief you about what to expect.
Court Attendance
Before you arrive at Court
Before you arrive at Court
    Tell Legal Services that you are required to attend court and provide a copy of the legal document/process (eg. Subpoena or Summons) requiring your attendance.

    • Dress neatly and conservatively. Clothing should be neat, clean and tidy.
    • Ensure you know which Court building and Court room you are attending and the time you are required to be there. The party who has called you as a witness will assist with this information.
    • Arrive at Court about 15 minutes early to ensure you are at the right location at the right time.
    • Do not take unnecessary items, especially dangerous objects like knives, scissors or aerosol cans etc.
At Court
At Court
    • Find your contact (usually the lawyer for the party who has called you as a witness) to confirm your attendance.
    • Witnesses are generally not allowed in Court hearings before they have given their evidence. You should remain outside the Court room, but close by, until you are specifically called to enter to give your evidence (called “testimony”).
    • The lawyers involved in the matter may provide some guidance on when you may be called.

    Courtroom etiquette
    Courtroom etiquette
      • Ensure your mobile phone is turned off (not just on silent as it may still make noise).
      • On entering and leaving make a slight bow to the Magistrate or Judge as a sign of respect to the court.
      • Do not discuss the matter being heard, what your evidence is, or any other issue connected with the case, unless you have confirmed it is appropriate to do so.
    Giving evidence
    Giving evidence
      • You will first be required to either take a religious oath on the Bible (or other religious book) or make an affirmation (non-religious) that you will tell the truth. Lying or knowingly misleading the court is a serious criminal offence known as perjury.
      • Both the oath and affirmation will be on a card in front of you and you will either be directed to read the relevant passage out to the Court or answer a question asked of you to confirm you will tell the truth.
      • Do not take notes while giving evidence. Simply concentrate on the questions directed to you and answer as fully as you can.
      • Taking photographs or video or audio recordings is strictly prohibited.
      • Judges and Magistrates should be addressed as “Your Honour”.

      Your testimony
      Your testimony
        • It is important to remember you are being called as a witness in your capacity as a TMR officer (not your personal capacity) so your answers should reflect TMR’s position or policy on the particular subject matter.
        • If you are requested for your personal view, you should provide this.
        • If you are asked a question that requires you to answer outside your position responsibilities then simply state that the issues are outside your area of responsibility and/or you cannot comment on TMR’s position or policy on the matter.
        • You may be called as a witness because you prepared a court certificate, report or similar document. You may request a copy of that document to read prior to or during your testimony to refresh your memory.

    On completion
    On completion
      • Witnesses are generally permitted to leave after giving evidence and are excused by the Judge or Magistrate. Once excused, witnesses may remain the Court room (in the public seating area) for the remainder of the hearing, unless the Judge or Magistrate directs otherwise. Spectators may leave at any time during the proceedings.
      • Unless there is a specific reason for you to stay, you should return to work when you are finished giving evidence.
      • Do not discuss the proceedings or your evidence with any member of the media.
