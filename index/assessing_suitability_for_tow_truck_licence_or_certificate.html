Assessing Suitability for Tow Truck Licence or Certificate (7442)

Assessing Criminal History
Purpose of assessment
Purpose of assessment

    A person’s criminal history can indicate whether the person has an appropriate regard for and understanding of the law, and whether they may pose a risk to the safety of others they must interact with to perform the function, and to the public or property.

    When assessing a criminal history useful questions to consider are:
    • Does this person have a high standard of conduct?
    • Are they safety conscious? Are they law abiding?
    • Would this person be considered an unacceptable safety risk to:
      • Others they come into contact with to perform their role including police and emergency services personnel
      • the public
      • property
Assessing offences
Assessing offences

    When assessing an applicant's criminal history, decision makers should consider the following factors:
    • types of offences and their relevance to the licence or certificate
    • when the offences occurred (recent offences are more relevant than earlier offences)
    • any pattern of re-offending including the frequency of committing offences
    • the person’s age at the time of the offences
    • type and severity of the penalty imposed (prison term, community order, fine)
    • degree to which the criminal history could adversely impact public confidence in the towing industry if a licence or certificate is granted, and
    • any other relevant facts or circumstances.
    Results that should not be taken into account
    Results that should not be taken into account

      The criminal history results - Acquitted, Acquittal, Charges withdrawn, Dismissed, Dismissed – no case to answer, No evidence to offer, Nolle prosequi, Not Guilty, Proceeding discontinued/withdrawn, Struck out, Withdrawn - are not taken into account when assessing suitability.

    Terminology commonly use in criminal offence history reports

      Orders Commonly used in criminal offence history reports
      OrderMeaning
      Absolutely dischargedThis is a finding of guilt, but with no punishment.
      Acquitted/AquittalCase heard – insufficient evidence – not guilty.
      Admonished and dischargedFound guilty but very minor punishment. [only in Children’s Court]
      Appeal dismissedAn appeal is when the offender asks a higher court to review a case in which they feel the verdict was wrong. When an appeal is dismissed it means the higher court upheld the original verdict.
      Charges withdrawnNot proceeded with. No finding of guilt.
      Community serviceThe court has ordered community service instead of jail time. The offender must report to a community corrections officer.

      The applicant may be requested to provide details of the community service conditions. These conditions are to be taken into account when assessing the application.

      ConvictedWhere a person accused of committing a criminal offence and is found guilty of that offence.
      Convicted and finedWhere a person accused of committing a criminal offence is found guilty of that offence. The court decides they must pay a sum of money (penalty) within a fixed period of time.
      Conviction recordedWhere a person accused of committing a criminal offence and is found guilty of that offence and a record of their guilt is recorded on their criminal history.
      Convicted and sentencedWhere a person accused of committing a criminal offence is guilty of that offence and the court decides what punishment is appropriate.
      Discharged under recognizanceFound guilty and ordered to pay nominated fee or to be of good behaviour for a period of time.
      DismissedEvidence has been heard, but the person is found not guilty.
      Dismissed – no case to answerEvidence has been heard and the prosecution has not been able to establish a case against the defendant. Meaning the defendant has no case to answer. The person is not guilty.
      First appearanceMatter is not finalised by the court. The alleged offender will return to court at later date for the Court to determine the charge
      Good behaviour bond A conviction is not usually recorded. There is no requirement to report to community corrections. If they re-offend, they could be brought back before the court but it normally does not happen.
      ImprisonmentWhere the court orders the offender is taken into custody at a prison for a specified period of time.
      Intensive correctional orderThe offender receives a custodial sentence, but in lieu of going to prison, they stay in the community under the supervision of a correctional officer. Under their orders, they may be directed to undertake specific counselling, training or courses etc.

      The applicant may be requested to provide details of the intensive correctional order conditions at the time of lodgement of the application. These conditions are to be taken into account when assessing the application.

      No conviction recordedThe offender has been charged with breaking the law and the court has found them guilty, or the court accepted the offender’s plea of guilty. The offender has still been convicted, but the court may opt to not record a conviction.
      No evidence to offerNot tested in court – The prosecution does not wish to proceed with the charge. The defendant is not convicted.
      No indictment presented-no true bill- proceeding discontinuedWhen a jury votes on a set of charges submitted by a prosecutor and decides there is not probable cause to support the charges, or that the charges should not be pursued for other reasons, they vote a "no true bill," which means no criminal case ensues. The defendant is not convicted.
      Nolle ProsequiNolle Prosequi is when there is insufficient evidence to proceed with a prosecution. The case has not been dismissed or withdrawn, instead, the charge remains un-finalised.
      Not guiltyEvidence heard – found not guilty
      ParoleNormally parole can be requested by the offender half way through any sentence. Depending on the circumstances of the case, a judge may, at the time of sentencing the offender, recommend parole at a certain period. If paroled, the offender is released from jail and is under the supervision of a correctional officer for the balance of the original sentence period.

      For example: A sentence of “10 years jail with parole after 3 years” means the offender would still be on parole for last 7 years of the sentence. They still fulfil the 10 year sentence, just not all of it in jail. If they re-offend, parole can be revoked and they can be returned to jail.

      Note: As a matter of policy Industry Authorities should not generally be granted to a person who is still on parole.

      ProbationProbation is generally given to young adults as a warning. Can be imposed for up to 4-5 years, regardless of whether a conviction is recorded or not. For the duration of their probation, the person is under the supervision of a community corrections officer. Conditions apply to a probation period. If they re-offend, they can be brought back before the court.

      The applicant may be requested to provide details of the probation at the time of lodgement of the application. These conditions are to be taken into account when assessing the application.

      Struck outCourt feels there is no case to answer or after hearing the prosecution case feels there is insufficient evidence to try the person.
      Suspended sentenceThe offender is given a sentence but they do not go to jail. The sentence is suspended for a period of time (normally longer than the recommended imprisonment period). If the person re-offends within the suspended sentence period, they could serve the initial sentence imposed in jail.
      WithdrawnNot tested in court – no outcome.
      Withdrawn dealt with ex-officioAppeared before court but solicitor requests case be heard directly by judge and jury. Becomes withdrawn from Magistrates court and goes direct to District or Supreme court. Dealt with in different court. Need to follow up outcome from District or Supreme hearing.

    Charges
    Charges

      Consideration must be given to charges that have not yet been dealt with by a court or withdrawn.
      It is important to remember that a charge is an allegation that has not yet been determined by a court and the person may be found not guilty.
      In assessing any charges, decision makers should consider:
      • the type of offence and its relevance to the licence or certificate
      • any pattern of re-offending, have they been convicted of a similar or related offence, and
      • any other relevant facts or circumstances.

    Assessment examples
    Assessment examples

      Examples of someone who is not appropriate based on criminal history:
      • Someone with offences for contravening a direction would not be an appropriate person to attend crash scene where they have to obey directions given by police;
      • Someone with offences for public nuisance, assault, unlawful possession of a weapon or violent crimes, as detailed in category 3, would not be appropriate to interact with vulnerable accident victims or emergency services personnel;
      • Someone with offences for unauthorised dealing with shop goods, theft, stealing and fraud would not be appropriate to hold towed vehicles and the personal property left inside them.

      Also see Examples of someone who is not appropriate based on traffic history

Assessing Traffic History
Purpose of Assessment
Purpose of Assessment

    Tow truck licence holders, drivers and assistants must be road safety conscious. They are expected to be responsible road users and to perform towing services in a professional and safe manner.

    A person’s traffic history can indicate whether they have appropriate regard for, and understanding of, the laws governing the use of Queensland roads. It may also indicate if they may pose a risk to their own safety and the safety of other road users and property.

Assessing offences
Assessing offences

    Generally only traffic offences committed within the past five years should be considered, with the more recent offences being the most relevant.

    Offences committed more than five years ago should only be considered if additional information is needed, such as whether offences within the past five years are part of a long term pattern of behaviour.

    Useful questions to consider are:

    • Is this person road safety conscious?
    • Do they obey the road rules?
    • Would a person with this traffic history be considered an unacceptable safety risk to the public and to property?

    Assessment examples

    Assessment examples

      Someone who is not appropriate based on traffic history:
      • Someone with a record of poor driving behaviour is not considered appropriate to hold an accreditation or licence as their driving behaviour demonstrates they are not road safety conscious and do not have a high standard of conduct. For this reason offences for speeding, dangerous driving, not obeying road rules, driving while under the influence, driving while unlicensed or suspended are not considered appropriate.
      • Granting an accreditation or licence to someone with poor driving behaviour could undermine the government’s administration of the tow truck scheme and negatively impact the reputation of the towing industry. The community have a high expectation that the government only issues licenses and accreditations to those who are appropriate.

      Also see Examples of someone who is not appropriate based on criminal history above.

Previous ConductIf a person has previously held a licence or certificate, consideration must be given to their conduct while performing regulated towing activities and whether they have previously had a licence or certificate cancelled or suspended.

When assessing the person’s previous conduct, decision makers should consider:

  • any complaints made about them
  • the nature and circumstances surrounding any complaints and the number of complaints
  • if their licence or certificate has been cancelled or suspended, when the action was taken and the reasons why it was taken, and
  • any other relevant facts or circumstances.

Consideration must also be given to the person’s capability to perform the activities associated with the relevant licence or certificate. If the decision maker is aware of any medical condition or physical or mental incapacity that may adversely affect the person ability to perform the activities, this must be considered in assessing whether they are an appropriate person to hold the certificate or licence.
